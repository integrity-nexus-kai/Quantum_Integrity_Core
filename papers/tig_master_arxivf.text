\begin{abstract}
We propose Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), a framework extending General Relativity by introducing a curvature-dependent integrity term derived from a spectral action. The additional contribution acts as a nonlinear feedback mechanism that suppresses high-curvature divergences. In the low-energy limit, General Relativity is recovered. We derive effective field equations, analyze the trace dynamics, and show that the model introduces a scalar degree of freedom with finite mass. A regular black hole solution and modified cosmological dynamics are discussed. The framework is presented as an effective theory, and testable predictions are outlined.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

General Relativity (GR) successfully describes gravitational phenomena but admits singular solutions and lacks intrinsic ultraviolet (UV) regularization. Various extensions introduce higher-order curvature terms or quantum corrections.

In this work, we explore an alternative approach motivated by spectral geometry, introducing an integrity functional that dynamically suppresses pathological configurations of spacetime.

\section{Spectral Framework}

We consider a spectral triple $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{H}, D)$, where $D$ is the Dirac operator encoding geometric information. The spectral action is extended by an additional functional:

\begin{equation}
S = \mathrm{Tr}\, f\left(\frac{D}{\Lambda}\right) + \lambda \mathrm{Tr}\,\log\left(1 + \frac{D^2}{\Lambda^2}\right)
\end{equation}

The second term introduces a nonlinear correction dependent on curvature.

\section{Effective Action}

In the low-energy limit, we consider the truncated effective action:

\begin{equation}
S_{\text{eff}} =
\frac{1}{16\pi G}
\int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left(
R + \frac{\alpha}{\Lambda^2} R^2
\right)
\end{equation}

This corresponds to an $f(R)$-type theory.

\section{Field Equations}

Variation yields:

\begin{equation}
G_{\mu\nu} + \mathcal{I}_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

with:

\begin{align}
\mathcal{I}_{\mu\nu}
&=
\frac{2\alpha}{\Lambda^2}R R_{\mu\nu}
-
\frac{\alpha}{2\Lambda^2}R^2 g_{\mu\nu}
\\
&+
\frac{2\alpha}{\Lambda^2}
\left(
g_{\mu\nu}\Box R
-
\nabla_\mu\nabla_\nu R
\right)
\end{align}

\section{Trace Equation}

Taking the trace:

\begin{equation}
-R + \frac{6\alpha}{\Lambda^2}\Box R = 8\pi G T
\end{equation}

which can be rewritten as:

\begin{equation}
\Box R - m^2 R = \frac{8\pi G \Lambda^2}{6\alpha} T
\end{equation}

with:

\begin{equation}
m^2 = \frac{\Lambda^2}{6\alpha}
\end{equation}

This corresponds to a propagating scalar degree of freedom.

\section{Black Hole Solution}

A regularized metric function of the form:

\begin{equation}
A(r) = 1 - \frac{2GM r^2}{r^3 + r_0^3}
\end{equation}

is consistent with the expected high-curvature suppression, replacing the singular core by a finite region.

\section{Cosmology}

For a flat FRW metric, the Ricci scalar is:

\begin{equation}
R = 6(2H^2 + \dot{H})
\end{equation}

The modified Friedmann equation includes additional curvature-dependent terms:

\begin{equation}
3H^2 = 8\pi G \rho + \frac{\alpha}{\Lambda^2}\left(\frac{1}{2}R^2 - 6H \dot{R}\right)
\end{equation}

These contributions become relevant in high-curvature regimes.

\section{Stability}

The model is stable under the conditions:

\begin{equation}
\alpha > 0, \quad \Lambda^2 > 0
\end{equation}

ensuring absence of tachyonic instabilities and ghost modes in the effective theory.

\section{Predictions}

The framework predicts:

\begin{itemize}
\item deviations from GR in strong gravity regimes
\item regular black hole cores
\item an additional scalar mode
\item modified early-universe dynamics
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}

TIG can be interpreted as an effective theory introducing curvature-dependent feedback. The spectral origin suggests a deeper geometric structure, but a full derivation remains open.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented TIG as a stability-driven extension of GR. The framework recovers GR in the low-energy limit and introduces controlled deviations at high curvature. Further work is required to fully derive the spectral action and confront observational constraints.
