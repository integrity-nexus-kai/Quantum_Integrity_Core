\begin{abstract}
We investigate Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a structural framework for horizon formation in quadratic $f(R)$ gravity with regularized mass profiles. Within a static, spherically symmetric setting, we show that the existence of horizons is governed by a critical transition controlled by a dimensionless parameter $\beta$.

In the TIG framework, horizon formation is not treated as a continuous deformation of a metric but as a structural transition encoded in the root structure of the horizon equation. The roots exhibit a saddle-node bifurcation at a critical value $\beta_c$, where the outer horizon branch terminates and the causal structure changes non-analytically.

The quadratic $f(R)$ sector introduces a scalar curvature scale that can be associated with the regularization radius of the effective geometry. Near criticality, the metric function develops an extended near-zero region, enhancing the tortoise coordinate and causing the echo delay time to scale as
\[
\Delta t_{\mathrm{echo}} \sim (\beta_c-\beta)^{-1/2}.
\]
This corresponds to the emergence of an effectively extended optical cavity.

Observable consequences include deviations in black hole shadow radii, quasi-normal-mode shifts, and characteristic gravitational-wave echo signatures. Unlike conventional regular black hole or exotic compact object models, TIG identifies horizon formation as a bifurcation phenomenon in the horizon equation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides an accurate description of gravitational phenomena across a wide range of scales \cite{einstein1915,wald1984}. Extensions such as $f(R)$ gravity introduce higher-curvature corrections and additional degrees of freedom \cite{sotiriou2010,defelice2010}.

Regular black hole geometries have been studied as alternatives to singular solutions \cite{bardeen1968,hayward2006}. Quasi-normal modes and gravitational-wave echoes provide observational probes of compact-object structure \cite{berti2009,konoplya2011,cardoso2016,cardoso2019}.

We define Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a framework in which spacetime configurations are classified by the qualitative root structure of the horizon equation. Distinct physical regimes correspond to different root multiplicities, and transitions occur when roots merge or disappear.

Here, the term ``topological'' refers to the qualitative structure of solution branches rather than to formal topological invariants. In this sense, TIG interprets horizon formation as a structural property of the geometry rather than a purely dynamical outcome.

The purpose of this work is to show that, within a quadratic $f(R)$ effective model, horizon formation is governed by a critical bifurcation structure. This provides a concrete realization of the TIG perspective and connects it to observable signatures.

\section{Quadratic $f(R)$ Framework}

We consider the action
\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}
\left(
R - 2\Lambda + \alpha R^2
\right)
\right]
+ S_{\mathrm{matter}} .
\end{equation}

Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2,
\qquad
\mu = 16\pi\alpha .
\end{equation}

The additional scalar curvature degree of freedom is
\begin{equation}
\phi = f'(R) = 1 + 2\mu R,
\end{equation}
with effective mass
\begin{equation}
m_\phi^2 = \frac{1}{3f''(R)} = \frac{1}{6\mu}.
\end{equation}

For $\mu>0$, this scalar mode is non-tachyonic. The scalar mass introduces a characteristic length scale
\begin{equation}
\ell_\phi \sim m_\phi^{-1} = \sqrt{6\mu}.
\end{equation}

We identify the regularization scale of the effective compact-object geometry with this scalar length scale,
\begin{equation}
r_c \sim \ell_\phi \sim \sqrt{6\mu}.
\end{equation}

Thus the dimensionless control parameter
\begin{equation}
\beta = \frac{r_c}{2M}
\end{equation}
is not arbitrary, but is tied to the strength of the quadratic curvature correction.

\section{Effective Geometry}

We consider a static, spherically symmetric metric
\begin{equation}
ds^2 =
-F(r)dt^2
+
\frac{dr^2}{F(r)}
+
r^2d\Omega^2 .
\end{equation}

The effective mass profile is chosen as
\begin{equation}
m(r)=\frac{Mr^3}{r^3+r_c^3},
\end{equation}
leading to
\begin{equation}
F(r)=1-\frac{2m(r)}{r}.
\end{equation}

This profile interpolates between a regular core at small radii and Schwarzschild behavior at large radii. It should be interpreted as an effective ansatz capturing the leading structural effect of curvature regularization, rather than as a complete exact solution of the full quadratic $f(R)$ field equations.

\section{Critical Horizon Structure}

Horizons satisfy
\begin{equation}
F(r_H)=0.
\end{equation}

Substituting the effective mass profile yields
\begin{equation}
r_H^3-2Mr_H^2+r_c^3=0.
\end{equation}

Introducing dimensionless variables
\begin{equation}
x=\frac{r_H}{2M},
\qquad
\beta=\frac{r_c}{2M},
\end{equation}
we obtain
\begin{equation}
x^3-x^2+\beta^3=0.
\end{equation}

The critical value is
\begin{equation}
\beta_c=\left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}.
\end{equation}

At $\beta=\beta_c$, two real roots merge. This is the characteristic signature of a saddle-node bifurcation. Below the critical value, horizon-bearing configurations exist; above it, the geometry becomes horizonless.

In the TIG framework, this marks a structural transition between distinct causal regimes. Horizon formation is therefore encoded in the root multiplicity of the horizon equation.

The collapse of the outer branch near $\beta_c$ indicates that the transition is not a smooth deformation of the Schwarzschild horizon. Instead, the number of admissible horizon roots changes discontinuously at the level of solution structure.

For small $\beta$, the outer horizon branch satisfies
\begin{equation}
x \simeq 1-\beta^3,
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}
r_H \simeq 2M(1-\beta^3).
\end{equation}

\section{Photon Sphere}

The photon sphere is determined by
\begin{equation}
rF'(r)-2F(r)=0.
\end{equation}

Near the critical regime, the deformation of $F(r)$ shifts the photon-sphere radius $r_{\mathrm{ph}}$. Since the photon sphere controls both the shadow radius and the eikonal quasi-normal-mode structure, the TIG transition can induce observable deviations in strong-field lensing and ringdown behavior.

The corresponding shadow radius is
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{sh}}=
\frac{r_{\mathrm{ph}}}
{\sqrt{F(r_{\mathrm{ph}})}} .
\end{equation}

\section{Echo Physics and Critical Scaling}

The characteristic echo delay is estimated by
\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\mathrm{echo}}
\simeq
2\int \frac{dr}{F(r)}.
\end{equation}

Near the critical point, the metric function develops an extended near-zero region. Locally, it can be expanded as
\begin{equation}
F(r) \approx a(r-r_c)^2+\epsilon,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\epsilon \sim \beta_c-\beta .
\end{equation}

The delay integral then scales as
\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\mathrm{echo}}
\sim
\int
\frac{dr}{(r-r_c)^2+\epsilon}
\sim
\epsilon^{-1/2}.
\end{equation}

Therefore,
\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\mathrm{echo}}
\sim
(\beta_c-\beta)^{-1/2}.
\end{equation}

This divergence is analogous to critical slowing down in dynamical systems and indicates the formation of an effectively extended optical cavity near the transition.

\section{Quasi-Normal Modes}

In the eikonal limit, quasi-normal modes are governed by photon-sphere properties \cite{berti2009,konoplya2011}. A schematic expression is
\begin{equation}
\omega_n
\approx
\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right)\Omega_{\mathrm{ph}}
-
i\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda_{\mathrm{ph}},
\end{equation}
where $\Omega_{\mathrm{ph}}$ is the photon-sphere angular frequency and $\lambda_{\mathrm{ph}}$ is the Lyapunov exponent controlling the instability of null geodesics.

In the TIG framework, both quantities are modified by the deformation of $F(r)$ near the critical regime. As the effective potential flattens, damping can decrease while the oscillation frequency shifts. A full numerical computation of the spectrum is left for future work.

\section{Observational Implications}

The magnitude of observable deviations depends sensitively on proximity to the critical regime $\beta\sim\beta_c$ and can become parametrically enhanced near the transition.

Observable consequences include:
\begin{itemize}
\item shadow-radius deviations,
\item quasi-normal-mode frequency shifts,
\item gravitational-wave echo signatures.
\end{itemize}

These channels provide possible probes of modified gravity in strong-field regimes \cite{berti2015test}. Unlike conventional regular black hole or exotic compact object models, the TIG framework identifies horizon formation as a bifurcation phenomenon in the horizon equation rather than as a consequence of boundary conditions or matter content alone.

\section{Stability and Limitations}

For $\mu>0$, the scalar curvature mode is non-tachyonic, supporting perturbative consistency at the scalar level.

However, the present analysis is based on an effective metric ansatz. Deriving the full solution from the complete quadratic $f(R)$ field equations remains an important direction for future work. A complete dynamical stability analysis and direct numerical computation of perturbative spectra are also left for subsequent work.

\section{Conclusion}

We identified a structurally controlled critical transition governing horizon formation in quadratic $f(R)$ gravity within the framework of Topological Integrity Gravity.

The transition separates horizon-bearing and horizonless configurations and is associated with non-analytic root behavior, photon-sphere shifts, quasi-normal-mode modifications, and divergent echo-delay scaling.

These results suggest that horizon formation may be understood as a structural bifurcation phenomenon in modified gravity rather than as a model-dependent feature of a particular compact-object ansatz.
