\begin{abstract}
We introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), a structurally constrained
framework for gravitational horizon formation. The central idea is that
admissible curvature corrections should not be treated as arbitrary
phenomenological deformations of General Relativity, but as structures
constrained by consistency requirements. Within a minimal static and spherically
symmetric reduction, these conditions lead to a cubic normal form governing
horizon formation. The resulting horizon equation exhibits a critical transition
separating configurations with horizons from compact configurations without
horizons. This transition is controlled by a dimensionless parameter and is
characterized by the coalescence of roots in the associated cubic equation. We
derive the critical parameter, analyze the discriminant structure, and discuss
the interpretation of the transition as a structural threshold rather than a
continuous perturbative deformation. The framework preserves the classical
Schwarzschild limit while defining a constrained high-curvature sector suitable
for further mathematical and observational analysis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides the most successful classical description of
gravitation. Its geometric formulation identifies gravitational interaction with
the curvature of spacetime and has passed a wide range of observational tests
since Einstein's formulation of the gravitational field equations
\cite{einstein1915,wald1984,carroll2004}. Nevertheless, the appearance of
singularities and the structure of horizon formation indicate that the classical
theory may not fully encode all structural constraints relevant at extreme
curvature scales.

A common strategy is to modify the gravitational action by adding curvature
corrections. Such models are often written as extensions of the Einstein-Hilbert
action through a generalized function \(f(R)\) of the Ricci scalar. These
approaches form a broad and well-studied class of modified gravitational
theories \cite{sotiriou2010,nojiri2011,faraoni2010}. However, this procedure may
introduce an overly large space of possible theories. If curvature corrections
are chosen freely, the resulting model space becomes phenomenological rather
than structurally constrained.

The purpose of this work is to formulate a minimal alternative viewpoint. We
assume that admissible curvature corrections are restricted by consistency
conditions. In this sense, the question is not merely which correction terms can
be written down, but which correction terms preserve the structural integrity of
horizon formation.

We call this framework \emph{Topological Integrity Gravity} (TIG). The term
``topological integrity'' is used here in a restricted mathematical sense: it
refers to the preservation of the qualitative root structure associated with
horizon formation under admissible curvature corrections.

The central result of this paper is the emergence of a cubic normal form,
\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0,
\label{eq:intro_cubic}
\end{equation}
where \(x\) is a dimensionless radial horizon variable and \(\beta\) is a
dimensionless structural control parameter. This equation exhibits a critical
transition: for sufficiently small \(\beta\), horizon solutions exist; beyond a
critical value, no positive horizon root remains.

The transition is not introduced by hand. It follows from the algebraic
structure of the reduced horizon equation. The resulting critical point is
therefore interpreted as a structural boundary between horizon and non-horizon
configurations. The associated root coalescence is structurally analogous to
fold-type criticality and saddle-node bifurcations known from catastrophe theory
\cite{arnold1992}.

\section{Geometric and Physical Setup}

We consider a four-dimensional spacetime \((M,g)\) equipped with a Lorentzian
metric \(g_{\mu\nu}\). The discussion is restricted to static, spherically
symmetric configurations. The general line element may be written in the form
\begin{equation}
ds^2 = -A(r)c^2dt^2 + B(r)dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2,
\label{eq:metric}
\end{equation}
where \(d\Omega^2\) denotes the metric on the unit sphere.

In the Schwarzschild solution of General Relativity, the horizon is located at
the radial coordinate
\begin{equation}
r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2},
\label{eq:schwarzschild_radius}
\end{equation}
where \(M\) is the mass of the central object \cite{schwarzschild1916}. The
Schwarzschild geometry remains the canonical reference solution for static black
holes in General Relativity \cite{wald1984,chandrasekhar1983}.

The horizon condition can be expressed through the vanishing of the lapse
function,
\begin{equation}
A(r)=0.
\label{eq:horizon_condition}
\end{equation}

In modified or structurally constrained theories, the functional form of
\(A(r)\) may receive corrections. The key question is whether such corrections
preserve a well-defined horizon structure.

\begin{definition}[Dimensionless horizon variable]
We define the dimensionless radial variable
\begin{equation}
x := \frac{r}{r_s}.
\label{eq:x_definition}
\end{equation}
The Schwarzschild horizon corresponds to \(x=1\).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Structural control parameter]
Let \(\ell\) denote a characteristic correction length scale associated with the
regularization or structural modification of the central region. We define the
dimensionless parameter
\begin{equation}
\beta := \frac{\ell}{r_s}.
\label{eq:beta_definition}
\end{equation}
The regime \(\beta \ll 1\) corresponds to corrections much smaller than the
Schwarzschild scale.
\end{definition}

The limit \(\beta=0\) must recover the classical Schwarzschild horizon. This
requirement imposes a normalization condition on any admissible horizon
equation: the dimensionless equation must admit \(x=1\) as the physical root
when \(\beta=0\).

\section{Structural Consistency Principle}

The guiding assumption of TIG is that gravitational corrections are not
arbitrary deformations. Instead, they must preserve the qualitative integrity of
the horizon formation problem.

\begin{definition}[Structural horizon consistency]
A horizon equation is structurally consistent if:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item it admits the Schwarzschild limit \(x=1\) when the correction parameter
vanishes;
\item it has a well-defined algebraic root structure;
\item horizon disappearance, if it occurs, takes place through a critical root
coalescence rather than by arbitrary removal of solutions;
\item the correction terms are dimensionless and compatible with the chosen
normalization;
\item the resulting transition can be analyzed through invariant algebraic
quantities such as the discriminant.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

These conditions are minimal. They do not specify a complete gravitational field
theory. Instead, they constrain the admissible reduced horizon equations.

The simplest nontrivial algebraic setting in which a horizon can disappear
through root coalescence is a cubic equation. A quadratic equation can describe
a pairwise disappearance of roots, but does not naturally encode the
Schwarzschild root together with a degenerate correction sector. The cubic
normal form is therefore the minimal algebraic structure capable of encoding:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item the Schwarzschild root at \(x=1\);
\item a degenerate zero-radius sector;
\item a finite correction scale;
\item a discriminant-controlled structural transition.
\end{enumerate}

This point is closely related to the normal-form viewpoint in local bifurcation
analysis, where qualitative changes of solution structure are captured by
minimal polynomial models \cite{arnold1992}.
