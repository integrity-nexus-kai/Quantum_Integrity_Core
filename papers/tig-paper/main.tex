\begin{abstract}
We introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), a structurally constrained
framework for gravitational horizon formation. The central idea is that
admissible curvature corrections should not be treated as arbitrary
phenomenological deformations of General Relativity, but as structures
constrained by consistency requirements. Within a minimal static and spherically
symmetric reduction, these conditions lead to a cubic normal form governing
horizon formation. The resulting horizon equation exhibits a critical transition
separating configurations with horizons from compact configurations without
horizons. This transition is controlled by a dimensionless parameter and is
characterized by the coalescence of roots in the associated cubic equation. We
derive the critical parameter, analyze the root structure, and discuss the
interpretation of the transition as a structural threshold rather than a
continuous perturbative deformation. The control parameter is interpreted as a
structural degree of freedom in the space of admissible solutions. For clarity,
we denote the associated structural coordinate by \(X_4\). This coordinate does
not represent an additional physical spatial dimension.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The framework is explicitly constructed such that the classical GR limit is recovered in the low-curvature regime.
General Relativity provides the most successful classical description of
gravitation. Its geometric formulation identifies gravitational interaction with
the curvature of spacetime and has passed a wide range of observational tests
since Einstein's formulation of the gravitational field equations
\cite{einstein1915,wald1984,carroll2004}. Nevertheless, the appearance of
singularities and the structure of horizon formation indicate that the classical
theory may not fully encode all structural constraints relevant at extreme
curvature scales.

A common strategy is to modify the gravitational action by adding curvature
corrections. Such models are often written as extensions of the Einstein-Hilbert
action through a generalized function \(f(R)\) of the Ricci scalar. These
approaches form a broad and well-studied class of modified gravitational
theories \cite{sotiriou2010,nojiri2011,faraoni2010}. However, this procedure may
introduce an overly large space of possible theories. If curvature corrections
are chosen freely, the resulting model space becomes phenomenological rather
than structurally constrained.

The purpose of this work is to formulate a minimal alternative viewpoint. We
assume that admissible curvature corrections are restricted by consistency
conditions. In this sense, the question is not merely which correction terms can
be written down, but which correction terms preserve the structural integrity of
horizon formation.

We call this framework \emph{Topological Integrity Gravity} (TIG). The term
``topological integrity'' is used here in a restricted mathematical sense: it
refers to the preservation of the qualitative root structure associated with
horizon formation under admissible curvature corrections.

The central result of this paper is the emergence of a cubic normal form,
\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0,
\label{eq:intro_cubic}
\end{equation}
where \(x\) is a dimensionless radial horizon variable and \(\beta\) is a
dimensionless structural control parameter. This equation exhibits a critical
transition: for sufficiently small \(\beta\), horizon solutions exist; beyond a
critical value, no positive horizon root remains.

The transition is not introduced by hand. It follows from the algebraic
structure of the reduced horizon equation. The resulting critical point is
therefore interpreted as a structural boundary between horizon and non-horizon
configurations. The associated root coalescence is structurally analogous to
fold-type criticality and saddle-node bifurcations known from catastrophe theory
\cite{arnold1992}.

A central interpretive point is that the parameter \(\beta\) should not be
understood as an additional physical spatial dimension. Rather, it defines an
additional structural degree of freedom in the space of admissible solutions.
We denote the corresponding structural coordinate by \(X_4\). This notation is
introduced only to name the organization direction in solution space; it is not
a reference to an additional spacetime dimension or to an external higher-
dimensional physical theory.
