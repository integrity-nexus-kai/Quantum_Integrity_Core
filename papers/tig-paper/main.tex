\begin{abstract}

We present a minimal formulation of Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a 
structural constraint on horizon formation within a quadratic $f(R)$ setting.

The central result is that, under consistency requirements on admissible curvature 
corrections, the horizon condition reduces to a cubic normal form. This structure 
exhibits a saddle-node bifurcation that governs the existence of horizons and 
introduces a critical parameter separating horizon and horizonless configurations.

In the limit of vanishing correction scale, the construction recovers the 
Schwarzschild solution of General Relativity, ensuring consistency with the 
classical theory.

The resulting critical behavior implies non-linear scaling near the transition 
point, arising directly from the imposed structural constraint rather than from 
phenomenological modification.

We discuss potential observational implications, including deviations in photon 
sphere structure, black hole shadow size, and near-critical echo delays, while 
emphasizing that these signatures are not unique discriminators at the present 
level of modeling.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides an accurate description of gravitational phenomena 
across a wide range of scales \cite{einstein1915,wald1984}. The structure and 
stability of horizons in static spacetimes are well understood within this framework.

Extensions such as $f(R)$ gravity introduce higher-curvature corrections and 
additional scalar degrees of freedom \cite{sotiriou2010,defelice2010}, enlarging 
the space of admissible solutions.

Regular black hole geometries provide examples where horizon structure is modified 
without introducing singularities \cite{bardeen1968,hayward2006}.

However, not all such solutions are physically consistent. Horizon formation must 
satisfy structural requirements, including regularity and stability under small 
perturbations.

In this work, we formulate a minimal structural constraint on horizon formation 
within a quadratic $f(R)$ setting. Rather than introducing a new gravitational 
theory, the TIG framework restricts admissible solutions through consistency 
conditions imposed on the horizon equation.

We show that these conditions lead to a cubic normal form exhibiting a saddle-node 
bifurcation, implying a critical parameter separating horizon-bearing and 
horizonless configurations. The Schwarzschild solution is recovered in the 
appropriate limit.

\section{Consistency Constraint}

The simplest structurally stable mechanism by which the number of real solutions 
changes is the saddle-node bifurcation. Its normal form is cubic, representing 
the minimal structurally stable transition.

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0
\label{eq:tig}
\end{equation}

where
\begin{equation}
x=\frac{r_H}{2M},
\qquad
\beta=\frac{r_c}{2M}.
\label{eq:dimensionless_variables}
\end{equation}

This equation is interpreted as a minimal structural constraint on horizon formation.

\section{Quadratic $f(R)$ Framework}

We consider
\begin{equation}
S=\int d^4x\sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}(R-2\Lambda+\alpha R^2)
\right]
+S_{\mathrm{matter}} .
\label{eq:fr_action}
\end{equation}

As a minimal effective realization consistent with the structural constraint, we use
\begin{equation}
F(r)=1-\frac{2Mr^2}{r^3+r_c^3}.
\label{eq:metric_function}
\end{equation}

This form reproduces the Schwarzschild lapse function in the limit $r_c\to 0$ and 
leads to the dimensionless horizon equation \eqref{eq:tig}.

This effective description is analogous in spirit to regular black hole constructions 
\cite{hayward2006}. It should not be interpreted as a complete solution of the 
quadratic $f(R)$ field equations in full generality, but as a reduced static sector 
used to implement and analyze the structural horizon constraint.

\section{Interpretation of the Scale $r_c$}

The parameter $r_c$ denotes the characteristic scale at which the structural 
constraint becomes relevant. In the dimensionless horizon equation it appears only 
through the ratio
\begin{equation}
\beta=\frac{r_c}{2M},
\end{equation}
which compares the internal correction scale to the Schwarzschild radius.

In the present formulation, $r_c$ should not be interpreted as an independently 
postulated horizon radius. Rather, it is an effective length scale controlling the 
deviation from the Schwarzschild geometry in the near-core region. The limit
\begin{equation}
r_c \to 0
\end{equation}
removes this correction and restores the classical Schwarzschild lapse.

For finite $r_c$, the metric function \eqref{eq:metric_function} replaces the 
central singular behavior by a regularized mass profile while leaving the 
asymptotic Schwarzschild form unchanged. The ratio $\beta$ therefore acts as the 
control parameter for the horizon transition.

Within the quadratic $f(R)$ setting, $r_c$ may be understood as an effective scale 
associated with higher-curvature corrections. A full derivation of this scale from 
the covariant field equations would require solving the modified equations with 
appropriate boundary and regularity conditions. This derivation is beyond the 
minimal static construction considered here.

\section{Effective Geometry}

We consider a static, spherically symmetric metric
\begin{equation}
ds^2=-F(r)dt^2+\frac{dr^2}{F(r)}+r^2d\Omega^2 .
\label{eq:metric}
\end{equation}

The corresponding effective mass profile is
\begin{equation}
m(r)=\frac{Mr^3}{r^3+r_c^3},
\label{eq:mass_profile}
\end{equation}
leading to
\begin{equation}
F(r)=1-\frac{2m(r)}{r}
=
1-\frac{2M r^2}{r^3+r_c^3}.
\end{equation}

This interpolates between a regular core and Schwarzschild asymptotics. The metric 
therefore provides a minimal realization of the TIG structural constraint in the 
static, spherically symmetric sector.

\section{Critical Horizon Structure}

For the effective geometry considered here, the horizon condition $F(r_H)=0$ 
reduces to Eq.~\eqref{eq:tig}.

The discriminant is
\begin{equation}
\Delta=\beta^3(4-27\beta^3).
\end{equation}

The physically relevant solution is the largest positive root, ensuring continuity 
with the Schwarzschild limit.

The critical point is given by
\begin{equation}
\beta_c=\left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3},
\qquad
x_c=\frac{2}{3}.
\label{eq:critical_point}
\end{equation}

For $\beta<\beta_c$, the cubic has three real roots, of which the positive roots 
correspond to horizon branches in the effective geometry. At $\beta=\beta_c$, two 
roots coincide, producing a degenerate horizon. For $\beta>\beta_c$, the physical 
horizon branch is absent.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:horizon-transition}, the horizon branches merge at the 
critical point.

\section{Photon Sphere Structure}

The photon sphere satisfies
\begin{equation}
rF'(r)-2F(r)=0,
\end{equation}
where $F(r)$ denotes the effective metric function.

In the Schwarzschild limit $r_c\to 0$, this condition gives
\begin{equation}
r_{\mathrm{ph}}^{\mathrm{GR}}=3M.
\end{equation}

For finite $r_c$, the photon sphere radius is obtained numerically. We define the 
relative deviation
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\mathrm{ph}}
=
\frac{r_{\mathrm{ph}}^{\mathrm{TIG}}-3M}{3M}.
\end{equation}

The deviation from the Schwarzschild value is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:photon_sphere}.

\section{Echo Physics}

The echo delay is estimated as
\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\mathrm{echo}} \sim \int \frac{dr}{F(r)},
\end{equation}
where $F(r)$ is the effective lapse function.

Near the critical point, the lapse develops a shallow minimum near the degenerate 
horizon. The scaling should therefore be interpreted as a local approximation in 
the near-critical region rather than as a complete waveform prediction.

The scaling behavior near criticality is illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:echo-delay}.

\section{TIG as a Structural Principle}

The analysis presented above suggests that the cubic horizon equation
\begin{equation}
f(x,\beta)=x^3-x^2+\beta^3=0
\end{equation}
should not be interpreted merely as a model-specific result, but as the minimal 
normal form of a structurally constrained horizon condition.

This motivates the following formulation:

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{TIG Structural Principle.} 
\textit{Admissible spacetime configurations are restricted by a structural 
consistency condition on horizon formation, such that the local horizon equation 
reduces, in an appropriate dimensionless representation, to a normal form 
exhibiting a structurally stable bifurcation.}
\medskip

In this perspective, the cubic equation arises as the universal representation of 
a saddle-node transition controlling the existence of horizons. The specific form 
of the metric function $F(r)$ is not fundamental, but provides a minimal realization 
of this constraint.

A fully covariant formulation of this principle would require embedding the 
structural condition into the spacetime field equations. In particular, one expects 
that admissible solutions satisfy a constraint of the schematic form
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}[g_{\mu\nu};r_c] = 0,
\label{eq:covariant_constraint}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{C}$ encodes a structural consistency condition on the geometry, 
and where $r_c$ enters as the scale at which the correction to the classical 
Schwarzschild structure becomes relevant.

In a covariant realization, $\mathcal{C}$ should be constructed from scalar 
quantities associated with the metric and curvature, so that the condition does 
not depend on a preferred coordinate representation. In the static, spherically 
symmetric sector, this constraint must reduce to the dimensionless cubic normal 
form \eqref{eq:tig}.

At the present level, the framework should therefore be understood as defining a 
restricted class of admissible solutions rather than a complete modification of 
the gravitational field equations.

This interpretation places TIG conceptually between phenomenological metric models 
and fully dynamical modified gravity theories: it introduces a nontrivial structural 
constraint while remaining agnostic about the underlying microscopic dynamics.

\section{Conclusion}

We have identified a minimal structural constraint on horizon formation leading 
to a cubic normal form with a saddle-node bifurcation.

The construction reproduces the Schwarzschild solution and remains compatible 
with General Relativity.

The cubic normal form should be understood as a minimal representation rather 
than a complete theory.

The scale $r_c$ enters as an effective correction scale controlling the departure 
from Schwarzschild behavior and the onset of the critical horizon transition.

A covariant completion of TIG would require an explicit construction of the 
constraint functional $\mathcal{C}[g_{\mu\nu};r_c]$ and its embedding into the 
field equations. The present work establishes the static, spherically symmetric 
sector in which the structural mechanism can be analyzed explicitly.

The results indicate that horizon formation may be governed by structural 
consistency conditions that introduce critical behavior. The observational 
signatures identified here remain non-unique and require further investigation.
