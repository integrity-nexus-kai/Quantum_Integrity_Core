\section{Introduction}

General Relativity was first formulated by Einstein~\cite{einstein1915} and remains the foundation of modern gravitational theory~\cite{wald1984}.

Among its extensions, $f(R)$ gravity has been extensively studied~\cite{defelice2010,sotiriou2010}, with the quadratic model originally proposed by Starobinsky~\cite{starobinsky1980}.

In this work, we investigate Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a constrained quadratic $f(R)$ framework and explore its implications for compact objects and observable signatures.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Formalism}

We consider the action:
\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}(R - 2\Lambda) + \alpha R^2
\right]

* S_{\mathrm{matter}}.
  \end{equation}

Defining
\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2, \quad \mu = 16\pi\alpha,
\end{equation}
the model corresponds to a quadratic $f(R)$ theory.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Field Equations}

The modified field equations are:
\begin{equation}
f'(R) R_{\mu\nu}

* \frac{1}{2} f(R) g_{\mu\nu}
* (g_{\mu\nu}\Box - \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu) f'(R)
  = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}.
  \end{equation}

with
\begin{equation}
f'(R) = 1 + 2\mu R.
\end{equation}

The trace equation reads:
\begin{equation}
3\Box f'(R) + f'(R)R - 2f(R) = 8\pi T.
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Static Configurations}

We consider static, spherically symmetric geometries:
\begin{equation}
ds^2 =

* F(r)dt^2

- \frac{dr^2}{F(r)}
- r^2 d\Omega^2.
  \end{equation}

\subsection{Effective Interior Profile}

While a full solution of the modified field equations remains beyond the scope of this work, the chosen ansatz captures the expected qualitative features of regularized compact objects in modified gravity scenarios.

The mass profile is given by:
\begin{equation}
m(r) = \frac{M r^3}{r^3 + r_c^3}.
\end{equation}

The metric function is:
\begin{equation}
F(r) = 1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}.
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Horizon Structure}

Horizons satisfy:
\begin{equation}
F(r_H) = 0.
\end{equation}

This leads to:
\begin{equation}
r_H^3 - 2M r_H^2 + r_c^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

Defining dimensionless variables:
\begin{equation}
x = \frac{r_H}{2M}, \quad \beta = \frac{r_c}{2M},
\end{equation}
we obtain:
\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Critical Transition}

The cubic equation exhibits a critical value:
\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}.
\end{equation}

For:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\beta \leq \beta_c$: horizons exist,
\item $\beta > \beta_c$: no real positive solution exists.
\end{itemize}

This transition separates black hole configurations from horizonless compact objects.

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Physical Interpretation of the Critical Transition}

The existence of a critical parameter $\beta_c$ suggests that horizon formation in this model is not a continuous process but is instead governed by a structural threshold.

For $\beta \leq \beta_c$, the geometry admits horizons and resembles standard black hole configurations. For $\beta > \beta_c$, no horizon forms despite the presence of a compact mass distribution.

This behavior indicates that the parameter $\beta$ effectively controls the balance between gravitational attraction and the regularizing effects introduced by the modified curvature structure.

In this sense, TIG introduces a mechanism by which classical horizon formation can be suppressed without requiring exotic matter, suggesting a geometrically driven transition between black holes and horizonless compact objects.

\section{Photon Sphere and Shadow Radius}

The photon sphere is defined by:
\begin{equation}
rF'(r) - 2F(r) = 0.
\end{equation}

This yields:
\begin{equation}
2x^6 - 3x^5 + 4\beta^3x^3 + 2\beta^6 = 0.
\end{equation}

The shadow radius is given by:
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{sh}} = \frac{r_{\mathrm{ph}}}{\sqrt{F(r_{\mathrm{ph}})}}.
\end{equation}

\section{Observational Implications}

The presence of a critical transition in the horizon structure implies potentially observable deviations from standard General Relativity predictions.

Near the critical regime $\beta \sim \beta_c$, the horizon radius decreases nonlinearly, which may lead to measurable deviations in black hole shadow sizes compared to classical expectations.

Similarly, gravitational lensing signatures could be modified due to changes in the effective photon sphere radius, particularly for compact objects close to the transition threshold.

In gravitational-wave observations, the absence of a horizon for $\beta > \beta_c$ may lead to altered ringdown signals or the appearance of echoes, providing a potential observational test of the model.

These effects suggest that TIG can, in principle, be constrained through high-precision astrophysical observations.

\section{Stability and Theoretical Consistency}

A full dynamical stability analysis of the solutions presented in this work remains beyond the scope of the current study. However, several qualitative arguments can be made regarding the consistency of the model.

First, the underlying $f(R)$ formulation with a positive quadratic correction ($\alpha > 0$) is known to avoid tachyonic instabilities and introduces a scalar degree of freedom with a well-defined mass scale~\cite{defelice2010,sotiriou2010}. This suggests that the theory itself is dynamically well-behaved at the perturbative level.

Second, the effective metric ansatz employed here leads to a regular interior geometry, avoiding curvature singularities typically present in classical black hole solutions. Such regular configurations are commonly associated with improved stability properties in modified gravity scenarios.

Third, the existence of a critical parameter $\beta_c$ separating horizon and horizonless configurations indicates that the solution space is partitioned into distinct regimes. This separation suggests that small perturbations are unlikely to continuously deform one class of solutions into the other, hinting at a form of structural stability.

Nevertheless, a complete analysis of linear perturbations, quasi-normal modes, and potential instabilities remains an important direction for future work.

\subsection{Relation to General Relativity and Regular Black Hole Models}

In the limit $\beta \to 0$, the model reduces to the Schwarzschild solution, recovering standard General Relativity in the absence of curvature corrections.

For finite $\beta$, the solutions exhibit qualitative similarities to regular black hole models such as the Bardeen or Hayward metrics, which also introduce modifications that remove central singularities.

However, in contrast to these phenomenological constructions, TIG is rooted in a modified gravitational action. The emergence of the critical transition is therefore not imposed by hand but arises from the interplay between the mass profile and the underlying curvature corrections.

This distinction suggests that TIG may provide a more structured framework for understanding regular compact objects within modified gravity.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

We have studied Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a constrained quadratic $f(R)$ gravity model and identified a critical parameter governing the existence of horizons.

The emergence of a critical value
\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}
indicates that horizon formation may be controlled by a structural threshold, separating black hole geometries from horizonless compact objects.

This result suggests that TIG provides a geometrically driven mechanism for regulating strong-gravity configurations without introducing exotic matter.

Future work should address the dynamical stability of these solutions and explore their full implications for astrophysical observations and gravitational-wave phenomenology.
