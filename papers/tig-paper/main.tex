\begin{abstract}
We introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), a structurally constrained
framework for gravitational horizon formation. The central idea is that
admissible curvature corrections should not be treated as arbitrary
phenomenological deformations of General Relativity, but as structures
constrained by consistency requirements. Within a minimal static and spherically
symmetric reduction, these conditions lead to a cubic normal form governing
horizon formation. The resulting horizon equation exhibits a critical transition
separating configurations with horizons from compact configurations without
horizons. This transition is controlled by a dimensionless parameter and is
characterized by the coalescence of roots in the associated cubic equation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The framework is explicitly constructed such that the classical GR limit is recovered in the low-curvature regime.

General Relativity provides the most successful classical description of gravitation.
Its geometric formulation identifies gravitational interaction with the curvature of spacetime.

The purpose of this work is to formulate a minimal structurally constrained viewpoint.
We assume that admissible curvature corrections are restricted by consistency conditions.

The central result of this paper is the emergence of a cubic normal form:

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0
\end{equation}

where \(x\) is a dimensionless radial horizon variable and \(\beta\) is a dimensionless structural control parameter.

\section{Geometric Setup}

We consider static spherically symmetric configurations with metric:

\begin{equation}
ds^2 = -A(r)c^2dt^2 + B(r)dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2
\end{equation}

The Schwarzschild radius is

\begin{equation}
r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}
\end{equation}

and we define the dimensionless horizon variable

\begin{equation}
x := \frac{r}{r_s}
\end{equation}

with structural parameter

\begin{equation}
\beta := \frac{\ell}{r_s}
\end{equation}

\section{Structural Consistency Principle}

The guiding assumption of TIG is that gravitational corrections are not arbitrary deformations.
Instead, they must preserve the qualitative integrity of the horizon formation problem.

\section{Derivation of the Cubic Normal Form}

Starting from the Schwarzschild-compatible polynomial:

\begin{equation}
P_0(x)=x^2(x-1)
\end{equation}

we obtain the expanded form

\begin{equation}
P_0(x)=x^3-x^2
\end{equation}

Introducing a finite structural correction yields:

\begin{equation}
x^3-x^2+\beta^3=0
\end{equation}

\section{Critical Structure}

The derivative is

\begin{equation}
\frac{dP}{dx}=3x^2-2x
\end{equation}

The nontrivial stationary point is

\begin{equation}
x_c=\frac{2}{3}
\end{equation}

and the critical parameter becomes

\begin{equation}
\beta_c=\left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

\section{Scaling Near Criticality}

Near the critical point:

\begin{equation}
\delta \sim \epsilon^{1/2}
\end{equation}

which is characteristic of saddle-node criticality.

\section{Observable Implications}

Possible implications include:

\begin{itemize}
\item black-hole shadow modifications,
\item photon-sphere deviations,
\item near-horizon lensing corrections,
\item gravitational-wave signatures near criticality.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

We introduced Topological Integrity Gravity as a structurally constrained approach to horizon formation.

The central horizon equation

\begin{equation}
x^3-x^2+\beta^3=0
\end{equation}

exhibits a critical transition separating horizon and non-horizon sectors.
