\section{Derivation of the Cubic Normal Form}
%=====================================================

The TIG horizon condition is constructed as the lowest-order algebraic normal form
capable of describing a transition between horizon-bearing and horizonless
configurations while preserving the Schwarzschild limit.

Let
\begin{equation}
x=\frac{r_H}{2M}, \qquad \beta=\frac{r_c}{2M},
\end{equation}
where $r_H$ is a candidate horizon radius, $M$ is the mass scale, and $r_c$ is a
correction scale. We seek a dimensionless algebraic horizon condition of the form
\begin{equation}
P(x,\beta)=0.
\end{equation}

The Schwarzschild limit requires that, for vanishing correction scale,
\begin{equation}
\beta \rightarrow 0,
\end{equation}
the physical horizon is recovered at
\begin{equation}
x=1.
\end{equation}
Therefore the reduced polynomial must contain the factor
\begin{equation}
x-1
\end{equation}
in the limit $\beta=0$.

A transition between two horizons and no horizons requires the coalescence of two
real roots. Algebraically, this is the defining condition of a saddle-node
bifurcation: at criticality one has
\begin{equation}
P(x_c,\beta_c)=0,
\qquad
\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}(x_c,\beta_c)=0.
\end{equation}
Thus, the minimal polynomial must be able to support a double root at finite
positive $x_c$.

A quadratic polynomial can produce a double root, but it cannot simultaneously
encode the Schwarzschild root at $x=1$ in the zero-correction limit together with
a separate degenerating pair of horizon roots without collapsing the structure
into a purely discriminant-level deformation. The next minimal possibility is
therefore a cubic polynomial.

We impose the following minimal requirements:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the polynomial is dimensionless;
\item the Schwarzschild horizon is recovered as $x=1$ when $\beta=0$;
\item the correction enters through the scale ratio $\beta$ only;
\item the polynomial admits a saddle-node degeneracy at finite $x>0$.
\end{enumerate}

The simplest cubic satisfying these requirements is
\begin{equation}
P(x,\beta)=x^3-x^2+\beta^3.
\label{eq:cubic_normal_form_derivation}
\end{equation}

Indeed, when $\beta=0$,
\begin{equation}
P(x,0)=x^3-x^2=x^2(x-1),
\end{equation}
so that the nonzero physical root is exactly
\begin{equation}
x=1.
\end{equation}

The double factor $x^2$ at the origin is not interpreted as a physical horizon.
Rather, it encodes the degenerate zero-radius sector that unfolds when the
correction scale becomes finite.

The power $\beta^3$ is fixed by dimensional consistency with the cubic polynomial
in $x$. Since both $x$ and $\beta$ are dimensionless ratios of length scales, the
lowest correction compatible with the cubic structure is proportional to
\begin{equation}
\beta^3.
\end{equation}

Thus, the TIG structural horizon equation is
\begin{equation}
x^3-x^2+\beta^3=0.
\label{eq:tig}
\end{equation}

This equation should be understood as a normal form, not as a fundamental field
equation. It captures the local algebraic structure of horizon creation and
annihilation in the same way that a saddle-node normal form captures the local
structure of root coalescence in dynamical systems.
