\begin{abstract}

We investigate Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a constrained quadratic $f(R)$ extension of General Relativity.

In the low-curvature regime, the model reduces to a Starobinsky-type theory. In contrast to phenomenological regular black hole constructions, curvature corrections are not introduced ad hoc but arise from a structurally constrained action.

We analyze static, spherically symmetric configurations exhibiting regular interiors and modified horizon structures. A central result is the emergence of a critical parameter governing horizon existence.

This transition is intrinsic to the theory and leads to nonlinear deviations in horizon size near the critical regime, providing potential observational signatures.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modified gravity theories based on $f(R)$ extensions provide a natural framework for exploring deviations from General Relativity.

In this work, we study Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a constrained quadratic $f(R)$ model.

Unlike regular black hole models such as the Bardeen or Hayward geometries, where regularity is imposed phenomenologically, the transition identified here emerges from the structure of the gravitational action itself.

This leads to the appearance of a critical parameter separating horizon-bearing and horizonless configurations.

\section{Formalism}

We consider the action:
\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g} \left[ \frac{1}{16\pi}(R - 2\Lambda + \alpha R^2) \right]
\end{equation}

Defining:
\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2, \quad \mu = 16\pi \alpha
\end{equation}

\section{Field Equations}

The field equations are:
\begin{equation}
f'(R) R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}f(R)g_{\mu\nu} - \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu f'(R) + g_{\mu\nu}\Box f'(R) = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\section{Static Configurations}

We consider static, spherically symmetric geometries.

\subsection{Effective Interior Profile}

The mass profile is chosen as:
\begin{equation}
m(r) = \frac{M r^3}{r^3 + r_c^3}
\end{equation}

This form ensures a regular de Sitter-like core for small $r$ and asymptotic Schwarzschild behavior for large $r$.

The metric function is:
\begin{equation}
F(r) = 1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Horizon Structure}

Horizons satisfy:
\begin{equation}
F(r_H) = 0
\end{equation}

which yields:
\begin{equation}
r_H^3 - 2M r_H^2 + r_c^3 = 0
\end{equation}

Defining dimensionless variables:
\begin{equation}
x = \frac{r_H}{2M}, \quad \beta = \frac{r_c}{2M}
\end{equation}

we obtain the cubic equation:
\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0
\end{equation}

\subsection{Critical Transition}

The cubic equation admits a critical value:
\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

For $\beta \leq \beta_c$, two horizons exist, while for $\beta > \beta_c$, no real positive solution remains.

This defines a sharp transition between black hole geometries and horizonless compact objects.

\section{Observational Implications}

Near the critical regime $\beta \sim \beta_c$, the horizon radius decreases nonlinearly.

This effect may lead to measurable deviations in black hole shadow sizes relative to standard General Relativity predictions.

In addition, the disappearance of horizons for $\beta > \beta_c$ suggests potential modifications in gravitational wave ringdown signals, including the possibility of echoes.

\section{Stability and Theoretical Consistency}

While a full dynamical analysis is beyond the present scope, the regular interior structure and absence of curvature singularities indicate improved stability properties compared to classical solutions.

\section{Conclusion}

We have analyzed Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a constrained quadratic $f(R)$ model and identified a critical parameter governing horizon formation.

The existence of a critical value:
\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

demonstrates that horizon formation is controlled by a structural threshold rather than a continuous process.

This suggests a geometrically driven mechanism for regulating strong-gravity configurations without introducing exotic matter.

Future work should address dynamical stability and explore observational consequences in more detail.
