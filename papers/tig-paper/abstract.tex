\begin{abstract}
We investigate a structurally controlled mechanism for horizon formation in quadratic $f(R)$ gravity with regularized mass profiles. Within a static, spherically symmetric framework, we show that the existence of horizons is not guaranteed by continuity but is instead governed by a critical transition controlled by a dimensionless parameter $\beta$. This parameter encodes the relative scale between the regular core and the Schwarzschild radius, and defines a sharp boundary between horizon-bearing and horizonless compact configurations.

The transition is traced to the algebraic structure of the horizon equation, whose roots exhibit a saddle-node bifurcation at a critical value $\beta_c$. At this point, the outer and inner horizon branches merge and annihilate, leading to a non-analytic change in the global causal structure. This establishes horizon formation as a structurally constrained phenomenon rather than a continuously deformable property of the metric.

We further analyze the near-critical regime and demonstrate that the metric function develops an extended near-zero plateau. This induces a strong enhancement of the tortoise coordinate and leads to a divergence of the echo delay time, $\Delta t_{\mathrm{echo}} \to \infty$ as $\beta \to \beta_c$. Physically, this corresponds to the emergence of an effectively infinite optical cavity, providing a geometric mechanism for long-lived gravitational-wave echoes without invoking exotic boundary conditions.

The framework naturally interpolates between classical black hole geometries and regular horizonless compact objects, while preserving asymptotic Schwarzschild behavior. Observable consequences include deviations in black hole shadow radii, shifts in quasi-normal mode spectra, and characteristic scaling laws for gravitational-wave echo signals near the critical regime.

These results suggest that critical horizon transitions may constitute a generic feature of regularized gravity models and provide a structurally grounded pathway for probing modifications of general relativity in the strong-field regime.
\end{abstract}
