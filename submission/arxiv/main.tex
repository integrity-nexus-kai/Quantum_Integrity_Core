\begin{abstract}
We analyze horizon formation in a quadratic $f(R)$ gravity model and identify a dimensionless parameter controlling the existence of horizons. Static, spherically symmetric configurations exhibit a critical threshold separating horizon-bearing from horizonless geometries.

The transition is derived within an effective description consistent with the scalar dynamics of quadratic $f(R)$ gravity. It leads to modifications in photon-sphere structure, quasi-normal-mode spectra, and signal propagation. Near the critical regime, deviations from Schwarzschild behavior become parametrically enhanced.

These results suggest that horizon formation in modified gravity can exhibit structurally controlled behavior, with potential implications for gravitational-wave and black hole imaging observations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides an accurate description of gravitational phenomena across a wide range of scales \cite{einstein1915,wald1984}. Nevertheless, extensions such as $f(R)$ gravity offer a minimal framework for incorporating higher-curvature corrections while preserving general covariance \cite{sotiriou2010,defelice2010}. The quadratic case is particularly important because of its relation to Starobinsky gravity \cite{starobinsky1980}.

A key question is whether horizon formation is structurally stable under such corrections. While classical black hole solutions possess well-defined event horizons, modified gravity theories can introduce additional degrees of freedom that alter this structure.

Regular black hole models such as those of Bardeen and Hayward replace the central singularity by an effective regular core \cite{bardeen1968,hayward2006}. In contrast, the present work focuses on the emergence of a critical parameter controlling the existence of horizons within an effective quadratic $f(R)$ framework.

The resulting phenomenology connects naturally to photon-sphere structure, quasi-normal modes, gravitational-wave ringdown, and black hole shadow observations \cite{berti2009,konoplya2011,cardoso2016,cardoso2019,abbott2016,eventhorizon2019}.
