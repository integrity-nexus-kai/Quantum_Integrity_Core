\begin{abstract}
Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) proposes a spectral-geometric framework for gravitational consistency. 
This work introduces a global integrity functional as a selection principle for admissible spacetime configurations. 
The framework extends General Relativity by incorporating global constraints that may affect black hole structure 
and cosmological evolution.
\end{abstract}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides a geometrical description of gravitation 
in terms of spacetime curvature \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. 
While highly successful, it does not impose a fundamental global constraint 
on admissible configurations.

Black hole thermodynamics suggests deeper links between geometry 
and information \cite{bekenstein1973, hawking1975}. 
Similarly, cosmological models remain dependent on parameterized inputs 
rather than structural necessity \cite{friedmann1922}.

These observations motivate the introduction of a global consistency principle. 
We propose Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), in which physically admissible configurations 
are selected by a global integrity condition rather than local dynamics alone.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Topological Integrity Formalism}

Let $(M,g)$ be a smooth Lorentzian manifold. 
We define an integrity functional:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}[g] = \int_M \mathcal{F}(R, \nabla R, \lambda_i)\, d\mu_g
\end{equation}

where $R$ denotes curvature invariants and $\lambda_i$ represent spectral quantities 
associated with geometric operators such as the Laplace-Beltrami operator.

A representative form is:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F} = \alpha R^2 + \beta \nabla_\mu R \nabla^\mu R + \gamma \sum_i \lambda_i
\end{equation}

Physical configurations satisfy:

\begin{equation}
\delta \mathcal{I}[g] = 0
\end{equation}

which defines a global consistency constraint.

\section{Field Equations}

Variation of the integrity functional yields modified field equations:

\begin{equation}
G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} + \mathcal{T}^{(\mathcal{I})}_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

where

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{T}^{(\mathcal{I})}_{\mu\nu} =
\frac{\delta \mathcal{I}}{\delta g^{\mu\nu}}
\end{equation}

encodes global integrity contributions.

In the limit $\mathcal{T}^{(\mathcal{I})}_{\mu\nu} \to 0$, 
standard General Relativity is recovered.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Black Hole Solutions}

For static, spherically symmetric configurations, 
the classical solution is given by the Schwarzschild metric \cite{schwarzschild1916}.

The modified vacuum equations read:

\begin{equation}
G_{\mu\nu} + \mathcal{T}^{(\mathcal{I})}_{\mu\nu} = 0
\end{equation}

Potential consequences include:

\begin{itemize}
\item suppression of curvature divergences,
\item constraints on horizon structure,
\item modified thermodynamic behavior.
\end{itemize}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Cosmology and Friedmann Dynamics}

We consider homogeneous and isotropic cosmological solutions 
described by the FLRW metric \cite{friedmann1922}.

The modified Friedmann equations take the form:

\begin{equation}
H^2 = \frac{8\pi}{3} \rho + \frac{\Lambda}{3} + \Delta_{\mathcal{I}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\frac{\ddot{a}}{a} = -\frac{4\pi}{3} (\rho + 3p) + \frac{\Lambda}{3} + \Pi_{\mathcal{I}}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta_{\mathcal{I}}$ and $\Pi_{\mathcal{I}}$ arise from global integrity constraints.

\section{Predictions and Observational Signatures}

The TIG framework becomes physically relevant if the integrity contribution 
leads to observable deviations from standard gravitational theory.

Expected qualitative effects include:

\begin{itemize}

\item Strong-field deviations near black holes, potentially observable 
in gravitational wave spectra.

\item Suppression or modification of classical singularities.

\item Corrections to black hole thermodynamics.

\item Effective contributions to cosmic expansion, possibly influencing 
late-time acceleration.

\item Spectral constraints on admissible spacetime geometries.

\end{itemize}

A simple phenomenological parametrization is:

\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\mathcal{I}}(a) = \delta_0 a^{-n}
\end{equation}

which allows comparison with observational data.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

Topological Integrity Gravity introduces a global constraint on admissible spacetime configurations. 
The framework extends General Relativity by incorporating spectral-geometric consistency conditions. 
Future work will focus on explicit realizations of the integrity functional and observational tests.
