\begin{abstract}
We propose a spectral-geometric framework in which physically admissible spacetime configurations are constrained by a global condition of topological integrity. In contrast to General Relativity, which determines gravitational dynamics through local curvature, the present approach introduces an integrity functional that acts as a global selection principle over geometries. We give a concrete realization of this functional through a quadratic curvature term and discuss its extension by spectral contributions. The resulting field equations contain explicit higher-curvature corrections and reduce to standard General Relativity in the appropriate limit. We discuss implications for black hole structure, cosmological evolution, and observational signatures.
\end{abstract}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides a geometrical description of gravitation in terms of spacetime curvature \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. While highly successful, it does not impose a fundamental global constraint on admissible configurations.

Black hole thermodynamics suggests deeper links between geometry and information \cite{bekenstein1973, hawking1975}. Similarly, cosmological models remain dependent on parameterized inputs rather than structural necessity \cite{friedmann1922}.

These observations motivate the introduction of a global consistency principle. We propose a framework in which physically admissible configurations are selected by a global integrity condition rather than local dynamics alone.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Topological Integrity Formalism}

Let $(M,g)$ be a smooth Lorentzian manifold. We define an integrity functional
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}[g] = \int_M \mathcal{F}\, d\mu_g .
\end{equation}

To make the framework explicit, we choose the minimal local integrity density
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{loc}} = \alpha R^2 ,
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the Ricci scalar and $\alpha$ is an integrity coupling parameter.

This term penalizes configurations with large curvature concentration and therefore acts as a global regularity constraint. A spectral extension may be written formally as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{spec}} = \gamma \, \zeta_{\Delta}(s),
\end{equation}
where $\zeta_{\Delta}(s)$ is a regularized spectral zeta function associated with a geometric operator such as the Laplace-Beltrami operator.

The total integrity functional is then
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}[g]
=
\int_M
\left(
\alpha R^2
+
\gamma \zeta_{\Delta}(s)
\right)
d\mu_g .
\end{equation}

In the present work, we focus on the local curvature contribution $\alpha R^2$, since it is explicitly variational and sufficient to generate concrete field equations.

Physical configurations satisfy
\begin{equation}
\delta \mathcal{I}[g] = 0 .
\end{equation}

\section{Field Equations}

We consider the total gravitational action
\begin{equation}
S[g]
=
\int_M
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}
(R - 2\Lambda)
+
\alpha R^2
\right]
d\mu_g
+
S_{\mathrm{matter}} .
\end{equation}

Variation with respect to the inverse metric $g^{\mu\nu}$ gives the modified field equations
\begin{equation}
G_{\mu\nu}
+
\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}
+
16\pi \alpha H_{\mu\nu}
=
8\pi T_{\mu\nu},
\end{equation}
where the integrity correction tensor is
\begin{equation}
H_{\mu\nu}
=
2 R R_{\mu\nu}
-
\frac{1}{2} g_{\mu\nu} R^2
-
2 \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu R
+
2 g_{\mu\nu} \Box R .
\end{equation}

Here $\Box = \nabla^\rho \nabla_\rho$ denotes the covariant d'Alembertian.

Thus the integrity contribution is no longer a black box. It is explicitly given by
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{T}^{(\mathcal{I})}_{\mu\nu}
=
16\pi \alpha H_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}

In the limit $\alpha \to 0$, the integrity correction vanishes and standard General Relativity is recovered.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Black Hole Solutions}

For static, spherically symmetric configurations, the classical solution is given by the Schwarzschild metric \cite{schwarzschild1916}. In vacuum, the modified field equations become
\begin{equation}
G_{\mu\nu}
+
16\pi \alpha H_{\mu\nu}
=
0 .
\end{equation}

Since the Schwarzschild exterior satisfies $R=0$, the local $R^2$ integrity term does not modify the exterior vacuum solution directly. This is an important consistency property: TIG preserves the classical weak-field Schwarzschild regime.

However, the correction becomes relevant when curvature scalars or their derivatives cease to be regular. In regimes where $R$ is dynamically generated by matter, quantum corrections, or non-vacuum effective stress, the tensor $H_{\mu\nu}$ can suppress divergent curvature behavior.

Possible consequences include:
\begin{itemize}
\item suppression of curvature divergences,
\item constraints on admissible interior black hole geometries,
\item modified collapse endpoints,
\item compatibility with the classical exterior solution.
\end{itemize}

\section{Cosmology and Friedmann Dynamics}

We consider homogeneous and isotropic cosmological solutions described by the FLRW metric \cite{friedmann1922}. In this case, the Ricci scalar is
\begin{equation}
R = 6 \left( \dot{H} + 2H^2 + \frac{k}{a^2} \right),
\end{equation}
where $H=\dot{a}/a$ is the Hubble parameter and $k$ is the spatial curvature parameter.

The modified Friedmann equations may be written schematically as
\begin{equation}
H^2
=
\frac{8\pi}{3}\rho
+
\frac{\Lambda}{3}
+
\Delta_{\mathcal{I}},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_{\mathcal{I}}$ arises from the $R^2$ integrity correction.

For the local integrity term, the correction scales with curvature and its time derivatives:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\mathcal{I}}
\sim
\alpha
\left(
R^2,
H\dot{R},
\ddot{R}
\right).
\end{equation}

This implies that integrity corrections are expected to be strongest in high-curvature cosmological regimes, such as the early universe, and suppressed in low-curvature late-time regimes unless enhanced by spectral or global contributions.

A phenomenological parametrization is
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\mathcal{I}}(a) = \delta_0 a^{-n},
\end{equation}
which allows comparison with observational data.

\section{Predictions and Observational Signatures}

The concrete $R^2$ realization of TIG leads to several qualitative predictions.

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Weak-field consistency:}
In vacuum regions with $R=0$, the local correction vanishes, preserving standard Schwarzschild behavior.

\item \textbf{High-curvature sensitivity:}
Integrity corrections become relevant when curvature scalars vary strongly, especially near collapse interiors or early-universe regimes.

\item \textbf{Singularity suppression:}
The quadratic curvature term penalizes divergent curvature configurations and may regularize otherwise singular solutions.

\item \textbf{Cosmological deviations:}
The modified Friedmann dynamics contain curvature-derivative corrections that may affect early-universe evolution.

\item \textbf{Spectral extension:}
A non-local spectral contribution may impose additional constraints on admissible spacetime geometries.

\end{itemize}

These predictions are falsifiable in principle through strong-field gravity, gravitational collapse models, and cosmological constraints on higher-curvature corrections.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Limitations}

The present work develops a minimal local realization of the TIG framework. Several limitations remain.

First, the spectral term $\zeta_{\Delta}(s)$ has been introduced only formally. A complete treatment requires specifying the operator, boundary conditions, regularization scheme, and physical interpretation of the spectral parameter $s$.

Second, the $R^2$ correction is not unique. Other curvature invariants such as $R_{\mu\nu}R^{\mu\nu}$, $R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}R^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}$, or topological densities may contribute to a more complete integrity functional.

Third, explicit black hole interior solutions and cosmological fits remain future work.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

Topological Integrity Gravity introduces a global constraint on admissible spacetime configurations. By choosing the explicit local integrity density $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{loc}}=\alpha R^2$, the framework yields concrete higher-curvature field equations rather than an unspecified correction term.

The resulting theory preserves standard General Relativity in the limit $\alpha \to 0$ and in Ricci-flat vacuum regions, while allowing deviations in high-curvature regimes. This makes TIG compatible with known weak-field gravitational tests while motivating further investigation into black hole interiors, early-universe cosmology, and spectral extensions of gravitational dynamics.
