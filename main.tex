\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides a geometrical description of gravitation in terms of spacetime curvature \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. 
While the theory is locally consistent and highly successful in describing gravitational phenomena, it does not impose a fundamental global constraint on the admissibility of physical configurations.

Developments in black hole thermodynamics suggest that geometric structures encode deeper informational or entropic properties \cite{bekenstein1973, hawking1975}. 
Similarly, cosmological models describe large-scale dynamics with increasing observational precision, yet remain dependent on parameterized inputs rather than structural necessity \cite{friedmann1922, planck2018}.

These observations indicate that current formulations of gravitational physics may be incomplete at the level of global consistency conditions. 
In particular, there is no explicit principle that enforces the coherence of geometric, dynamical, and informational aspects within a unified framework.

In this work, we introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), a spectral-geometric framework that postulates a fundamental constraint on admissible physical configurations. 
Rather than modifying the local dynamics of General Relativity, TIG imposes a global condition of topological integrity, which acts as a selection principle over physically realizable states.

This approach reframes gravitational theory from a purely dynamical description to a constrained geometric system, in which consistency itself becomes a primary physical quantity. 
We develop the formal structure of this framework and explore its implications for black hole solutions and cosmological dynamics.
