\begin{abstract}
We introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), a quadratic $f(R)$ modification of General Relativity characterized by an $R^2$ curvature correction. We derive the modified field equations and analyze static, spherically symmetric solutions. The model admits regular interior configurations and exhibits a critical integrity scale that determines the existence of horizons. Below this scale, solutions resemble Schwarzschild black holes, while above it, horizonless compact objects emerge. We discuss the relation of the model to established $f(R)$ gravity frameworks and outline potential observational signatures.
\end{abstract}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

General Relativity (GR) provides a highly successful description of gravitation \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. Nevertheless, extensions of GR are widely studied in the context of high-energy physics and cosmology.

Among these, $f(R)$ gravity represents a minimal and well-established modification \cite{defelice2010, sotiriou2010}, where the Einstein-Hilbert action is generalized to a function of the Ricci scalar.

In this work, we introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a quadratic $f(R)$ model and analyze its implications for black hole structure. While $R^2$ corrections are well known, we focus on their impact on horizon formation and identify a critical scale governing the transition between black holes and horizonless compact objects.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Formalism}

\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}(R - 2\Lambda) + \alpha R^2
\right]
+ S_{\mathrm{matter}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2, \quad \mu = 16\pi\alpha
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Field Equations}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) R_{\mu\nu}
- \frac{1}{2} f(R) g_{\mu\nu}
+ (g_{\mu\nu}\Box - \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu) f'(R)
= 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) = 1 + 2\mu R
\end{equation}

\section{Relation to $f(R)$ Gravity}

The model belongs to the class of quadratic $f(R)$ theories. It is well known that such theories introduce an additional scalar degree of freedom with mass scale $m^2 \sim 1/\alpha$.

For $\alpha > 0$, the theory avoids tachyonic instabilities and is dynamically stable \cite{defelice2010}. In the low-curvature regime, GR is recovered.

\section{Black Hole Solutions}

We consider static, spherically symmetric configurations.

\subsection{Interior Ansatz}

\begin{equation}
ds^2 =
-F(r)dt^2
+
\frac{dr^2}{F(r)}
+
r^2 d\Omega^2
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
F(r) = 1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
m(r) = \frac{M r^3}{r^3 + r_c^3}
\end{equation}

This ansatz interpolates between Schwarzschild behavior at large radii and a regular core at small radii.

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Horizon Equation}

\begin{equation}
r_H^3 - 2M r_H^2 + r_c^3 = 0
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
x = \frac{r_H}{2M}, \quad \beta = \frac{r_c}{2M}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Critical Transition}

\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

For $\beta \leq \beta_c$, horizons exist.

For $\beta > \beta_c$, no real positive root exists and the configuration is horizonless.

This defines a transition between black hole and non-black-hole states.

\section{Observational Implications}

The presence of a critical scale implies deviations from GR near the horizon scale.

Near $\beta_c$, the horizon radius is reduced, affecting strong-field observables such as lensing and quasi-normal modes. For $\beta > \beta_c$, compact objects without horizons may form.

These features could be tested with gravitational wave observations and black hole imaging.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Cosmological Considerations}

Quadratic curvature corrections modify the early-time dynamics of the universe.

At late times, the model reduces effectively to $\Lambda$CDM, ensuring compatibility with current cosmological observations.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

We presented TIG as a quadratic $f(R)$ extension of General Relativity.

A key result is the emergence of a critical integrity scale that governs horizon formation:

\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

This provides a simple mechanism for transitioning between black holes and horizonless compact objects within a minimal modification of GR.
