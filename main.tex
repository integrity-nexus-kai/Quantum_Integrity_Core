\section{Topological Integrity Formalism}

We introduce the concept of topological integrity as a global constraint on admissible geometric configurations. 
Let $(M, g)$ be a smooth Lorentzian manifold representing spacetime. 
We define an integrity functional $\mathcal{I}[g]$ that assigns a scalar measure of consistency to a given configuration.

The functional $\mathcal{I}[g]$ is assumed to depend on spectral and geometric properties of the manifold, including curvature invariants and global topological structure. 
In general form, we write:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}[g] = \int_M \mathcal{F}(R, \nabla R, \lambda_i, \dots)\, d\mu_g
\end{equation}

where $R$ denotes curvature invariants, $\nabla R$ their derivatives, and $\lambda_i$ represent spectral quantities associated with the geometry.

Physical configurations are postulated to satisfy an extremal condition on $\mathcal{I}[g]$:

\begin{equation}
\delta \mathcal{I}[g] = 0
\end{equation}

This condition defines a selection principle over admissible spacetime geometries, analogous to an action principle but interpreted as a constraint on global consistency rather than local dynamics.

In this framework, classical gravitational solutions correspond to configurations that satisfy both the Einstein field equations and the integrity condition. 
Deviations from standard solutions arise when the integrity constraint imposes additional structure beyond local curvature dynamics.
