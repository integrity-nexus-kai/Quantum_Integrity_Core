\begin{abstract}
Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) is a modified gravitational theory based on a quadratic curvature correction to General Relativity. In its minimal realization, the theory corresponds to an $R^2$ extension of the gravitational action, leading to well-defined higher-curvature field equations while preserving classical vacuum solutions. We derive the modified field equations, analyze their implications for black hole configurations, and obtain an explicit cosmological prediction. The framework remains consistent with known weak-field limits while introducing dynamically activated corrections in evolving curvature regimes.
\end{abstract}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides a geometrical description of gravitation in terms of spacetime curvature \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. Modified gravity theories introduce higher-order curvature terms to account for high-energy and cosmological regimes.

In this work, we present Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) in its minimal realization, corresponding to a quadratic curvature extension.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Formalism}

\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}(R - 2\Lambda) + \alpha R^2
\right]
+ S_{\mathrm{matter}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2, \quad \mu = 16\pi\alpha
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Field Equations}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) R_{\mu\nu}
- \frac{1}{2} f(R) g_{\mu\nu}
+ (g_{\mu\nu}\Box - \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu) f'(R)
= 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) = 1 + 2\mu R
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Limit Analysis}

\textbf{GR limit:}
\begin{equation}
\alpha \to 0 \Rightarrow G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Vacuum limit:}
\begin{equation}
R = 0 \Rightarrow \text{Schwarzschild solution preserved}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Weak-field limit:}

Linearizing around Minkowski space yields standard Newtonian gravity plus small corrections of order $\alpha$.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Black Hole Solutions}

In vacuum ($T_{\mu\nu}=0$) and $R=0$, classical Schwarzschild solutions remain valid. Deviations arise only in non-Ricci-flat or dynamical curvature regimes.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Cosmology}

FLRW metric:

\begin{equation}
ds^2 = -dt^2 + a(t)^2 d\vec{x}^2
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
R = 6(\dot{H} + 2H^2)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
H^2 =
\frac{
8\pi \rho + \Lambda + 8\pi\alpha R^2 - 48\pi\alpha H \dot{R}
}{
3(1 + 32\pi\alpha R)
}
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Explicit Cosmological Prediction}

\begin{equation}
\dot{R} = 6(\ddot{H} + 4H\dot{H})
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
3H^2 =
8\pi\rho + \Lambda
+
288\pi\alpha
\left(
\dot{H}^2 - 2H^2\dot{H} - H\ddot{H}
\right)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Prediction:}
Cosmological deviations arise from time variation of curvature rather than curvature magnitude.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Stability Analysis}

The minimal $R^2$ model corresponds to a well-known subclass of $f(R)$ gravity.

For $\alpha > 0$, the additional scalar degree of freedom is non-tachyonic, and ghost instabilities are avoided at the classical level \cite{defelice2010}.

Higher-derivative extensions are not considered here.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Observational Consistency}

\textbf{Gravitational waves:}
Ricci-flat vacuum solutions remain unchanged, ensuring compatibility with LIGO observations.

\textbf{Cosmology:}
Deviations are expected primarily in early-universe regimes and may be constrained by CMB data \cite{planck2018}.

\textbf{Expansion history:}
Modified Friedmann dynamics provide a testable deviation from $\Lambda$CDM.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

We presented TIG as a quadratic curvature extension of General Relativity. The theory yields explicit field equations and a concrete cosmological prediction.

The framework preserves classical limits while introducing dynamical curvature corrections, providing a testable extension of standard gravitational theory.
