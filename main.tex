\begin{abstract}
We introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), a quadratic $f(R)$ modification of General Relativity characterized by an $R^2$ curvature correction. We derive the modified field equations and analyze static, spherically symmetric solutions. The model admits regular interior configurations that arise dynamically from the modified gravitational action and exhibits a critical integrity scale that determines the existence of horizons. Below this scale, solutions resemble Schwarzschild black holes, while above it, horizonless compact objects emerge. We further derive the photon-sphere condition and shadow-radius prediction, providing a direct observable signature of the model. We discuss the relation of TIG to established $f(R)$ gravity frameworks and outline possible astrophysical tests.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

General Relativity (GR) provides a highly successful description of gravitation \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. Nevertheless, extensions of GR are widely studied in the context of high-energy physics and cosmology.

Among these, $f(R)$ gravity represents a minimal and well-established modification \cite{defelice2010, sotiriou2010}, where the Einstein-Hilbert action is generalized to a function of the Ricci scalar.

In this work, we introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a quadratic $f(R)$ model and analyze its implications for black hole structure and observables.

\section{Formalism}

\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}(R - 2\Lambda) + \alpha R^2
\right]
+ S_{\mathrm{matter}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2, \quad \mu = 16\pi\alpha
\end{equation}

\section{Field Equations}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) R_{\mu\nu}
- \frac{1}{2} f(R) g_{\mu\nu}
+ (g_{\mu\nu}\Box - \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu) f'(R)
= 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) = 1 + 2\mu R
\end{equation}

\section{Relation to $f(R)$ Gravity}

The model belongs to the class of quadratic $f(R)$ theories. Such theories introduce an additional scalar degree of freedom with mass scale $m^2 \sim 1/\alpha$.

For $\alpha > 0$, the additional scalar mode is non-tachyonic, and ghost instabilities are avoided \cite{defelice2010}. In the low-curvature regime, GR is recovered.

\section{Black Hole Solutions}

We consider static, spherically symmetric configurations.

\subsection{Interior Ansatz}

\begin{equation}
ds^2 =
-F(r)dt^2
+
\frac{dr^2}{F(r)}
+
r^2 d\Omega^2
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
F(r) = 1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
m(r) = \frac{M r^3}{r^3 + r_c^3}
\end{equation}

This ansatz interpolates between Schwarzschild behavior at large radii and a regular core at small radii.

A natural scale is
\begin{equation}
r_c \sim \sqrt{\alpha}.
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Horizon Equation}

\begin{equation}
r_H^3 - 2M r_H^2 + r_c^3 = 0
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
x = \frac{r_H}{2M}, \quad \beta = \frac{r_c}{2M}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Critical Integrity Scale}

\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

Horizons exist only for $\beta \leq \beta_c$, while for $\beta > \beta_c$ the configuration becomes horizonless.

\section{Photon Sphere and Shadow Radius}

The photon sphere is determined by
\begin{equation}
rF'(r) - 2F(r) = 0.
\end{equation}

In dimensionless form:
\begin{equation}
2x^6 - 3x^5 + 4\beta^3x^3 + 2\beta^6 = 0.
\end{equation}

The shadow radius is:
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{sh}} = \frac{r_{\mathrm{ph}}}{\sqrt{F(r_{\mathrm{ph}})}}.
\end{equation}

Thus, TIG predicts deviations in shadow size controlled by $\beta$.

\section{Observational Implications}

The modified horizon and photon-sphere structure can in principle be tested via black hole imaging, gravitational lensing, and gravitational-wave observations.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Comparison with Regular Black Hole Models}

Regular black hole solutions such as the Bardeen and Hayward metrics introduce phenomenological modifications to remove singularities.

In contrast, TIG arises from a modified gravitational action within the $f(R)$ framework. The regular structure and critical transition follow from the underlying dynamics.

A key distinction is the existence of the critical scale $\beta_c$, which governs a transition between black hole and horizonless states.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Cosmological Considerations}

Quadratic curvature corrections modify early-universe dynamics while reducing to $\Lambda$CDM at late times.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented TIG as a quadratic $f(R)$ extension of General Relativity.

A key result is the existence of a critical integrity scale
\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3},
\end{equation}
which defines a transition between black hole and horizonless compact objects.
