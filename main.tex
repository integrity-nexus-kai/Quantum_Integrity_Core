\begin{abstract}
We introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), a modified gravitational theory based on a quadratic curvature extension of General Relativity. In its minimal realization, the model corresponds to an $R^2$ correction to the Einstein-Hilbert action. We derive the modified field equations and analyze static, spherically symmetric solutions. The model admits regular black hole interiors and predicts a critical integrity scale that governs the existence of horizons. Below this scale, solutions reduce to Schwarzschild-like black holes, while above it, horizonless compact objects emerge. We further discuss cosmological implications and observational signatures. The theory preserves standard low-curvature limits while introducing dynamically activated corrections in high-curvature regimes.
\end{abstract}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides a geometrical description of gravitation in terms of spacetime curvature \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. Modified gravity theories introduce higher-order curvature terms to address high-energy and cosmological regimes.

We present Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a quadratic $f(R)$ extension and analyze its implications for black hole structure and cosmology.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Formalism}

\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}(R - 2\Lambda) + \alpha R^2
\right]
+ S_{\mathrm{matter}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2, \quad \mu = 16\pi\alpha
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Field Equations}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) R_{\mu\nu}
- \frac{1}{2} f(R) g_{\mu\nu}
+ (g_{\mu\nu}\Box - \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu) f'(R)
= 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) = 1 + 2\mu R
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Limit Analysis}

\textbf{GR limit:}
\begin{equation}
\alpha \to 0 \Rightarrow G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Vacuum limit:}
\begin{equation}
R = 0 \Rightarrow \text{Schwarzschild solution preserved}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Weak-field limit:}

Linearizing around Minkowski space yields Newtonian gravity with corrections of order $\alpha$.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Black Hole Solutions}

In vacuum ($T_{\mu\nu}=0$) and $R=0$, classical Schwarzschild solutions remain valid.

In regions of high curvature, the TIG correction modifies the internal structure of compact objects.

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Regular Black Hole Interior Ansatz}

We consider a static spherically symmetric metric

\begin{equation}
ds^2 =
-F(r)dt^2
+
\frac{dr^2}{F(r)}
+
r^2 d\Omega^2
\end{equation}

with

\begin{equation}
F(r) = 1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}
\end{equation}

and

\begin{equation}
m(r) = \frac{M r^3}{r^3 + r_c^3}
\end{equation}

For large $r$:

\begin{equation}
m(r) \to M
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
F(r) \to 1 - \frac{2M}{r}
\end{equation}

For small $r$:

\begin{equation}
m(r) \approx \frac{M}{r_c^3} r^3
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
F(r) \approx 1 - \frac{2M}{r_c^3} r^2
\end{equation}

This corresponds to a de-Sitter-like core with finite curvature.

A natural scale is

\begin{equation}
r_c \sim \sqrt{\alpha}
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Horizon Structure}

The horizon radius is determined by

\begin{equation}
F(r_H)=0,
\end{equation}

leading to

\begin{equation}
r_H^3 - 2M r_H^2 + r_c^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

Introducing dimensionless variables

\begin{equation}
x = \frac{r_H}{2M}, \qquad \beta = \frac{r_c}{2M},
\end{equation}

we obtain

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Critical Integrity Scale}

The critical transition occurs when the cubic polynomial develops a degenerate root.

\begin{equation}
P(x)=x^3-x^2+\beta^3
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\frac{dP}{dx}=3x^2-2x=0
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
x_c=\frac{2}{3}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\beta_c^3=\frac{4}{27}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\beta_c=\left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

Horizons exist only for $\beta \leq \beta_c$. For $\beta > \beta_c$, the solution becomes horizonless.

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Numerical Horizon Prediction}

The outer horizon radius corresponds to the largest real root of

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Observational Signatures}

The modified horizon structure leads to measurable deviations from General Relativity.

Near the critical scale, the horizon radius is reduced relative to the Schwarzschild value, affecting gravitational redshift, lensing, and ringdown signals. For $\beta > \beta_c$, horizonless compact objects may replace classical black holes.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Cosmology}

\begin{equation}
ds^2 = -dt^2 + a(t)^2 d\vec{x}^2
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
R = 6(\dot{H} + 2H^2)
\end{equation}

The modified Friedmann equation introduces higher-order curvature corrections that become relevant at early times. At late times, the model approaches standard $\Lambda$CDM behavior.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

We presented TIG as a quadratic curvature extension of General Relativity with a modified horizon structure.

A key result is the existence of a critical integrity scale

\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

which defines a transition between black hole and horizonless compact objects.
