\begin{abstract}
Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) is a modified gravitational theory based on a quadratic curvature correction to General Relativity. In its minimal realization, the theory corresponds to an $R^2$ extension of the gravitational action. We derive the modified field equations, analyze black hole configurations, and present explicit cosmological predictions. The theory preserves classical limits while introducing dynamically activated corrections in high-curvature regimes.
\end{abstract}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides a geometrical description of gravitation in terms of spacetime curvature \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. Modified gravity theories introduce higher-order curvature terms to address high-energy and cosmological regimes.

We present Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) in its minimal realization as a quadratic curvature extension.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Formalism}

\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}(R - 2\Lambda) + \alpha R^2
\right]
+ S_{\mathrm{matter}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2, \quad \mu = 16\pi\alpha
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Field Equations}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) R_{\mu\nu}
- \frac{1}{2} f(R) g_{\mu\nu}
+ (g_{\mu\nu}\Box - \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu) f'(R)
= 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) = 1 + 2\mu R
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Limit Analysis}

\textbf{GR limit:}
\begin{equation}
\alpha \to 0 \Rightarrow G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Vacuum limit:}
\begin{equation}
R = 0 \Rightarrow \text{Schwarzschild solution preserved}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Weak-field limit:}

Linearizing around Minkowski space yields Newtonian gravity with corrections of order $\alpha$.

\section{Black Hole Solutions}

In vacuum ($T_{\mu\nu}=0$) and $R=0$, classical Schwarzschild solutions remain valid.

In regions of high curvature, the TIG correction may modify the internal structure of black holes.

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Regular Black Hole Interior Ansatz}

We consider a static spherically symmetric metric

\begin{equation}
ds^2 =
-F(r)dt^2
+
\frac{dr^2}{F(r)}
+
r^2 d\Omega^2
\end{equation}

with

\begin{equation}
F(r) = 1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}
\end{equation}

and

\begin{equation}
m(r) = \frac{M r^3}{r^3 + r_c^3}
\end{equation}

For large $r$:

\begin{equation}
m(r) \to M
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
F(r) \to 1 - \frac{2M}{r}
\end{equation}

For small $r$:

\begin{equation}
m(r) \approx \frac{M}{r_c^3} r^3
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
F(r) \approx 1 - \frac{2M}{r_c^3} r^2
\end{equation}

This corresponds to a de-Sitter-like core with finite curvature.

A natural scale is

\begin{equation}
r_c \sim \sqrt{\alpha}
\end{equation}

The horizon radius is determined by the condition

\begin{equation}
F(r_H)=0,
\end{equation}

which yields

\begin{equation}
r_H^3 - 2M r_H^2 + r_c^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

Introducing the dimensionless variables

\begin{equation}
x = \frac{r_H}{2M}, \qquad \beta = \frac{r_c}{2M},
\end{equation}

the horizon equation becomes

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

Thus, the regular interior ansatz leads to a modified horizon structure controlled by the dimensionless integrity scale $\beta$.

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Existence of Horizons}

The existence of real, positive solutions of the cubic equation

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0
\end{equation}

determines whether a black hole horizon exists.

For sufficiently small $\beta$, the equation admits a real positive root, corresponding to a modified event horizon.

However, beyond a critical value $\beta_c$, no real positive solution exists. This implies that horizon formation is suppressed and the object becomes a horizonless compact configuration.

Thus, TIG predicts a transition between black hole and non-black-hole states controlled by the integrity scale.

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Critical Integrity Scale}

The critical value of the integrity scale is obtained by analyzing the minimum of

\begin{equation}
P(x)=x^3-x^2+\beta^3.
\end{equation}

The stationary points satisfy

\begin{equation}
\frac{dP}{dx}=3x^2-2x=x(3x-2)=0.
\end{equation}

The relevant positive stationary point is

\begin{equation}
x_c=\frac{2}{3}.
\end{equation}

At the critical transition, the minimum touches zero:

\begin{equation}
P\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)=0.
\end{equation}

This yields

\begin{equation}
\beta_c^3=\frac{4}{27},
\end{equation}

and therefore

\begin{equation}
\beta_c=\left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
=
\frac{\sqrt[3]{4}}{3}.
\end{equation}

Thus, horizons exist only for

\begin{equation}
\beta \leq \beta_c.
\end{equation}

For

\begin{equation}
\beta > \beta_c,
\end{equation}

the regular compact object becomes horizonless.

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Observational Signatures}

The modified horizon structure leads to distinct observational consequences.

For $\beta \ll \beta_c$, deviations from General Relativity are negligible and classical black hole behavior is recovered.

As $\beta \rightarrow \beta_c$, the horizon radius decreases relative to the Schwarzschild value, leading to a compact object with modified gravitational redshift and lensing properties.

For $\beta > \beta_c$, no event horizon forms. The object behaves as a horizonless ultra-compact configuration, which may be distinguishable from classical black holes via:

\begin{itemize}
\item Absence of a true shadow edge
\item Modified ringdown signals in gravitational wave observations
\item Deviations in strong lensing near the compact object
\end{itemize}

Thus, TIG predicts observable deviations in the near-horizon regime that could be tested with current and future astrophysical observations.
