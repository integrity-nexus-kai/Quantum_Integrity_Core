\begin{abstract}
Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) is a modified gravitational theory based on a quadratic curvature correction to General Relativity. In its minimal realization, the theory corresponds to an $R^2$ extension of the gravitational action, leading to well-defined higher-curvature field equations while preserving classical vacuum solutions. We derive the modified field equations, analyze their implications for black hole configurations, and obtain an explicit cosmological prediction in the form of a modified Friedmann equation. The resulting framework remains compatible with known weak-field limits while introducing dynamically activated corrections in evolving curvature regimes.
\end{abstract}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

General Relativity provides a geometrical description of gravitation in terms of spacetime curvature \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. While highly successful, it is expected to receive corrections at high curvature scales.

Modified gravity models, including $f(R)$ theories, introduce higher-order curvature terms to address cosmological and strong-field phenomena.

In this work, we introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) in its minimal realization, corresponding to a quadratic curvature extension. The theory provides a controlled modification of Einstein gravity with explicit field equations and well-defined limits.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Topological Integrity Formalism}

We consider the gravitational action

\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}(R - 2\Lambda) + \alpha R^2
\right]
+ S_{\mathrm{matter}}.
\end{equation}

This corresponds to an $f(R)$ theory with

\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2,
\end{equation}

where $\mu = 16\pi \alpha$.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Field Equations}

Variation with respect to $g^{\mu\nu}$ yields

\begin{equation}
f'(R) R_{\mu\nu}
- \frac{1}{2} f(R) g_{\mu\nu}
+ (g_{\mu\nu}\Box - \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu) f'(R)
= 8\pi T_{\mu\nu},
\end{equation}

with

\begin{equation}
f'(R) = 1 + 2\mu R.
\end{equation}

In the limit $\alpha \to 0$, standard Einstein field equations are recovered.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Black Hole Solutions}

In vacuum, $T_{\mu\nu} = 0$. For Ricci-flat solutions ($R=0$), the correction terms vanish.

Thus, classical Schwarzschild solutions are preserved in the exterior region. Deviations may arise in regions where curvature becomes dynamically relevant.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Cosmology and Friedmann Dynamics}

We consider a spatially flat FLRW metric

\begin{equation}
ds^2 = -dt^2 + a(t)^2 d\vec{x}^2.
\end{equation}

The Ricci scalar is

\begin{equation}
R = 6(\dot{H} + 2H^2).
\end{equation}

The modified Friedmann equation reads

\begin{equation}
H^2 =
\frac{
8\pi \rho + \Lambda + 8\pi\alpha R^2 - 48\pi\alpha H \dot{R}
}{
3(1 + 32\pi\alpha R)
}.
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Explicit Cosmological Prediction}

For the FLRW background,

\begin{equation}
R = 6(\dot{H} + 2H^2),
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\dot{R} = 6(\ddot{H} + 4H\dot{H}).
\end{equation}

Substituting yields

\begin{equation}
H^2 =
\frac{
8\pi\rho + \Lambda
+
288\pi\alpha
\left(
\dot{H}^2 + 4H^4 - H\ddot{H}
\right)
}{
3\left[
1 + 192\pi\alpha(\dot{H} + 2H^2)
\right]
}.
\end{equation}

Equivalently,

\begin{equation}
3H^2 =
8\pi\rho + \Lambda
+
288\pi\alpha
\left(
\dot{H}^2 - 2H^2\dot{H} - H\ddot{H}
\right).
\end{equation}

In the limit $\alpha \to 0$:

\begin{equation}
3H^2 = 8\pi\rho + \Lambda.
\end{equation}

The correction terms vanish for constant curvature but become relevant when the Hubble parameter evolves dynamically.

\begin{quote}
Cosmological deviations from General Relativity are governed by time derivatives of curvature rather than curvature alone.
\end{quote}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Limitations}

The present work considers only the minimal $R^2$ realization. Extensions involving higher-derivative terms may introduce additional degrees of freedom and require separate stability analysis.

Spectral or non-local generalizations are not included in this formulation.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

We have presented Topological Integrity Gravity as a minimal quadratic curvature extension of General Relativity. The theory yields explicit modified field equations while preserving classical vacuum solutions.

A key result is the explicit cosmological prediction showing that deviations arise dynamically through curvature evolution. This provides a concrete and testable direction for further investigation.
