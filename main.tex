\begin{abstract}
We introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG), a quadratic $f(R)$ modification of General Relativity characterized by an $R^2$ curvature correction. We derive the modified field equations and analyze static, spherically symmetric solutions. The model admits regular interior configurations and exhibits a critical integrity scale that determines the existence of horizons. Below this scale, solutions resemble Schwarzschild black holes, while above it, horizonless compact objects emerge. We further derive the photon-sphere condition and shadow-radius prediction, providing a direct observable signature of the model. We discuss the relation of TIG to established $f(R)$ gravity frameworks and outline possible astrophysical tests.
\end{abstract}

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

General Relativity (GR) provides a highly successful description of gravitation \cite{einstein1915, wald1984}. Nevertheless, extensions of GR are widely studied in the context of high-energy physics, black hole interiors, and cosmology.

Among these, $f(R)$ gravity represents a minimal and well-established modification \cite{defelice2010, sotiriou2010}, where the Einstein-Hilbert action is generalized to a function of the Ricci scalar.

In this work, we introduce Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) as a quadratic $f(R)$ model and analyze its implications for black hole structure. While $R^2$ corrections are well known, we focus on their impact on horizon formation, photon-sphere structure, and compact-object observables.

\section{Formalism}

\begin{equation}
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}
\left[
\frac{1}{16\pi}(R - 2\Lambda) + \alpha R^2
\right]
+ S_{\mathrm{matter}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f(R) = R - 2\Lambda + \mu R^2, \quad \mu = 16\pi\alpha
\end{equation}

\section{Field Equations}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) R_{\mu\nu}
- \frac{1}{2} f(R) g_{\mu\nu}
+ (g_{\mu\nu}\Box - \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu) f'(R)
= 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
f'(R) = 1 + 2\mu R
\end{equation}

\section{Relation to $f(R)$ Gravity}

The model belongs to the class of quadratic $f(R)$ theories. Such theories introduce an additional scalar degree of freedom with mass scale $m^2 \sim 1/\alpha$.

For $\alpha > 0$, the additional scalar mode is non-tachyonic, and ghost instabilities are avoided \cite{defelice2010}. In the low-curvature regime, GR is recovered.

\section{Black Hole Solutions}

We consider static, spherically symmetric configurations.

\subsection{Interior Ansatz}

\begin{equation}
ds^2 =
-F(r)dt^2
+
\frac{dr^2}{F(r)}
+
r^2 d\Omega^2
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
F(r) = 1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
m(r) = \frac{M r^3}{r^3 + r_c^3}
\end{equation}

This ansatz interpolates between Schwarzschild behavior at large radii and a regular core at small radii.

For large $r$,

\begin{equation}
m(r) \to M,
\qquad
F(r) \to 1 - \frac{2M}{r}.
\end{equation}

For small $r$,

\begin{equation}
m(r) \approx \frac{M}{r_c^3}r^3,
\end{equation}

and therefore

\begin{equation}
F(r) \approx 1 - \frac{2M}{r_c^3}r^2.
\end{equation}

This corresponds to a de-Sitter-like core with finite curvature.

A natural scale is

\begin{equation}
r_c \sim \sqrt{\alpha}.
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Horizon Equation}

The horizon radius is determined by

\begin{equation}
F(r_H)=0.
\end{equation}

Using the mass function above, this gives

\begin{equation}
r_H^3 - 2M r_H^2 + r_c^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

Introducing dimensionless variables

\begin{equation}
x = \frac{r_H}{2M}, \qquad \beta = \frac{r_c}{2M},
\end{equation}

we obtain

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Critical Integrity Scale}

The critical transition occurs when the cubic polynomial develops a degenerate root.

\begin{equation}
P(x)=x^3-x^2+\beta^3
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\frac{dP}{dx}=3x^2-2x=0
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
x_c=\frac{2}{3}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\beta_c^3=\frac{4}{27}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\beta_c=\left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

Horizons exist only for $\beta \leq \beta_c$. For $\beta > \beta_c$, the solution becomes horizonless.

% --------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Numerical Horizon Structure}

The outer horizon radius corresponds to the largest real root of

\begin{equation}
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0.
\end{equation}

\section{Photon Sphere and Shadow Radius}

For a static, spherically symmetric metric of the form

\begin{equation}
ds^2 =
-F(r)dt^2
+
\frac{dr^2}{F(r)}
+
r^2 d\Omega^2,
\end{equation}

the photon sphere is determined by the condition

\begin{equation}
rF'(r)-2F(r)=0.
\end{equation}

Using the dimensionless variable

\begin{equation}
x=\frac{r}{2M},
\end{equation}

the TIG metric function becomes

\begin{equation}
F(x)=1-\frac{x^2}{x^3+\beta^3}.
\end{equation}

The photon-sphere condition then yields

\begin{equation}
2x^6 - 3x^5 + 4\beta^3x^3 + 2\beta^6 = 0.
\end{equation}

For $\beta=0$, the Schwarzschild result is recovered:

\begin{equation}
x_{\mathrm{ph}}=\frac{3}{2},
\end{equation}

corresponding to

\begin{equation}
r_{\mathrm{ph}}=3M.
\end{equation}

The apparent shadow radius is given by the critical impact parameter

\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{sh}}
=
\frac{r_{\mathrm{ph}}}{\sqrt{F(r_{\mathrm{ph}})}}.
\end{equation}

In dimensionless form,

\begin{equation}
\frac{R_{\mathrm{sh}}}{2M}
=
\frac{x_{\mathrm{ph}}}{\sqrt{F(x_{\mathrm{ph}})}}.
\end{equation}

Thus, TIG predicts deviations in the photon-sphere radius and shadow radius controlled by the integrity scale $\beta$.

\section{Observational Implications}

The presence of a critical scale implies deviations from GR near the horizon scale.

Near $\beta_c$, the horizon radius is reduced relative to the Schwarzschild value, affecting gravitational redshift, lensing, and ringdown signals. The photon-sphere equation further implies that the shadow radius is modified relative to the Schwarzschild value.

For $\beta > \beta_c$, compact horizonless objects may form. Such objects could be tested through black hole imaging, strong lensing measurements, and gravitational-wave ringdown observations.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Cosmological Considerations}

Quadratic curvature corrections modify the early-time dynamics of the universe.

At late times, the model reduces effectively to $\Lambda$CDM, ensuring compatibility with current cosmological observations.

% --------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

We presented TIG as a quadratic $f(R)$ extension of General Relativity.

A key result is the emergence of a critical integrity scale that governs horizon formation:

\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}.
\end{equation}

This provides a simple mechanism for transitioning between black holes and horizonless compact objects within a minimal modification of GR.

We further derived the photon-sphere condition and shadow-radius prediction, establishing a direct observational channel through which TIG may be tested.
