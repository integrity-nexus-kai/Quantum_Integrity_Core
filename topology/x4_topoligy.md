- bounded horizon formation
- recursive geometric consistency
- topological state transitions
- integrity-preserving spacetime evolution
- future X4 formalization

The topology layer acts as the bridge between:

- the cubic integrity constraint

# 2. MOTIVATION

The original TIG formulation introduces a bounded horizon condition through the cubic relation:

\[
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0
\]

The discriminant structure separates admissible from non-admissible geometric states.

This implies that horizon formation is not merely metric-dependent, but structurally constrained by the topology of admissible state space itself.

The topology layer therefore attempts to answer:

- Which geometric transitions are allowed?
- Which states remain structurally stable?
- Which transitions violate integrity constraints?
- How does spacetime preserve recursive consistency?

# 3. PRELIMINARY INTERPRETATION OF X4

X4 is introduced as a structural coordinate describing admissible recursive geometric configuration space.

At the current stage, X4 is not interpreted as:

- an ordinary spatial coordinate
- a classical extra dimension
- a compactified Kaluza-Klein dimension

Instead, X4 represents:

> the topological state coordinate governing recursive structural admissibility.

This interpretation implies that spacetime evolution may depend not only on metric curvature, but also on the integrity state of recursive geometric configuration.

# 6. STRUCTURAL INTERPRETATION OF HORIZONS

In standard General Relativity, horizons emerge through metric evolution alone.

In TIG, horizons appear only if:

- metric conditions
AND
- topological integrity conditions

are simultaneously satisfied.

This introduces the possibility that:

- some classical singularities become topologically inaccessible
- collapse may terminate before horizon completion
- bounded high-curvature states may exist

The topology layer therefore acts as a structural regulator on spacetime evolution.
