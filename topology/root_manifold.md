# 1. PURPOSE

This document introduces the preliminary root manifold interpretation of the Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) framework.

The objective is to investigate the geometric organization of solution branches generated by the TIG cubic equation.

The root manifold acts as the structural transition space connecting:

- admissible horizon states,
- critical transition configurations,
- and non-horizon compact states.

---

# 2. MOTIVATION

The original TIG formulation is based on the cubic relation:

\[
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0
\]

The equation generates multiple roots depending on the value of:

\[
\beta
\]

The structure of these roots changes near the discriminant boundary:

\[
\Delta = \beta^3(4 - 27\beta^3)
\]

This suggests that the solution space itself possesses an underlying geometric organization.

The root manifold is introduced as a first structural interpretation of this organization.

---

# 3. ROOT SPACE

Each admissible solution of the cubic equation corresponds to a point within root space.

The totality of admissible roots forms a connected geometric structure.

This structure is referred to as the root manifold.

The manifold organizes:

- branch continuity,
- critical transitions,
- admissibility flow,
- and recursive structural evolution.

---

# 4. BRANCH STRUCTURE

The cubic equation generates multiple solution branches.

These branches may represent distinct geometric regimes.

Examples include:

- horizon-supporting configurations,
- bounded compact states,
- critical transition states,
- and forbidden collapse regimes.

The root manifold describes how these branches connect and separate.

# 5. CRITICAL TRANSITION REGION

Near the discriminant boundary:

\[
\Delta = 0
\]

multiple roots coalesce.

This produces a critical transition region within the manifold.

The transition region separates:

- stable admissible evolution,
- from structurally forbidden configurations.

The critical surface therefore acts as a topology-changing boundary within root space.

---

# 6. ADMISSIBILITY FLOW

Movement through the manifold corresponds to geometric evolution across admissible states.

An admissible transition:

- preserves recursive consistency,
- remains within the allowed topology region,
- and avoids forbidden singular transitions.

The manifold therefore constrains physical evolution geometrically.
