# STABILITY ATTRACTORS
## Recursive Stability Structures in TIG Topology

Version 0.1  
Status: Foundational Draft

---

# 1. PURPOSE

This document defines the preliminary concept of stability attractors within the Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) topology framework.

Stability attractors describe structural regions toward which recursive geometric evolution naturally tends under admissibility-preserving dynamics.

The objective is to formalize:

- stable recursive configurations,
- bounded topology-preserving evolution,
- equilibrium transition behavior,
- and integrity-stabilizing geometric structures.

---

# 2. MOTIVATION

Previous TIG topology documents define:

- admissible states,
- recursive dynamics,
- integrity metrics,
- transition operators,
- and integrity collapse.

However, no framework yet exists for describing:

> why some recursive configurations remain structurally stable over time.

Stability attractors are introduced to define preferred recursive stability regions within admissible state space.

---

# 3. CORE PRINCIPLE

Within TIG:

> recursive geometric evolution is not random.

Admissible recursive systems may naturally evolve toward structurally stable regions preserving:

- recursive consistency,
- topology continuity,
- bounded evolution,
- and integrity stability.

These regions are referred to as stability attractors.

---

# 4. BASIC INTERPRETATION

A stability attractor is a region of admissible state space toward which recursive evolution converges.

Symbolically:

\[
S_n \rightarrow A_s
\]

where:

- \(S_n\) denotes recursive state evolution,
- and \(A_s\) denotes a stability attractor.

Attractors therefore represent recursively preferred structural configurations.

---

# 5. PRIMARY ATTRACTOR TYPES

Several preliminary attractor classes are proposed.

---

## 5.1 Stable Horizon Attractors

These attractors preserve admissible horizon-supporting configurations.

Properties include:

- bounded recursion,
- topology continuity,
- and stable integrity metrics.

---

## 5.2 Compact Stable Attractors

These attractors correspond to bounded non-horizon compact states.

Possible properties include:

- regularized curvature behavior,
- bounded topology structure,
- and recursive stabilization near criticality.

---

## 5.3 Critical Equilibrium Attractors

These attractors exist near critical transition regions.

Near:

\[
\Delta = 0
\]

recursive evolution may temporarily stabilize around critical configurations before divergence or stabilization occurs.

---

## 5.4 Forbidden Repulsors

Forbidden regions may behave as recursive repulsors rather than attractors.

Recursive evolution preserving admissibility naturally avoids:

- topology fracture,
- recursive inconsistency,
- and integrity collapse.

---

# 6. ATTRACTOR STABILITY

A stability attractor remains admissible only if integrity preservation continues across recursive evolution.

Symbolically:

\[
\mathcal{I}(A_s) > 0
\]

Attractors losing recursive consistency may destabilize into collapse regions.

---

# 7. RELATION TO RECURSIVE DYNAMICS

Recursive dynamics define evolution through admissible state space.

Stability attractors define regions toward which admissible recursion converges.

Thus:

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| recursive dynamics | define movement |
| stability attractors | define stable convergence |

Attractors therefore organize long-term recursive behavior.

---

# 8. RELATION TO ROOT MANIFOLD

The root manifold defines continuous geometric structure.

Stability attractors define preferred regions within the manifold.

The manifold therefore contains:

- stable regions,
- critical regions,
- unstable regions,
- and forbidden regions.

---

# 9. RELATION TO TRANSITION OPERATORS

Transition operators may either:

- preserve attractor stability,
- weaken attractor coherence,
- or trigger instability transitions.

Admissible operators tend to maintain recursive attraction behavior.

Collapse-inducing operators destabilize attractor structure.

---

# 10. RELATION TO CRITICAL SURFACES

Critical surfaces define regions of elevated transition sensitivity.

Near criticality:

\[
\Delta = 0
\]

attractor stability may weaken or bifurcate.

Critical surfaces therefore act as attractor transition boundaries.

---

# 11. RELATION TO X4

X4 parametrizes recursive traversal through structural state space.

Within this interpretation:

- X4 defines traversal direction,
- attractors define stable convergence regions.

Some X4 trajectories may converge toward stable admissible configurations.

Others may approach instability or collapse.

---

# 12. RELATION TO GENERAL RELATIVITY

General Relativity primarily describes metric evolution without explicit recursive attractor structure.

TIG topology extends this framework by introducing:

- recursive stability regions,
- admissibility-preserving convergence,
- and topology-constrained equilibrium structure.

Attractors therefore represent a higher-order recursive stability layer acting on geometric evolution.

---

# 13. RELATION TO SIR

Stability attractors provide one of the strongest structural bridges between TIG topology and Structural Integrity Recursion (SIR).

Within SIR:

- recursive integrity stabilizes system evolution.

Within TIG:

- attractors stabilize admissible geometric recursion.

This suggests that recursive stability may represent a universal structural convergence principle.

Within TIG topology:
