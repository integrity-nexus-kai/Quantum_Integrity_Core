Draft 1.0

# 1. Purpose

This document defines the current minimal admissibility assumptions used within the TIG framework (Topological Integrity Gravity).

The objective is not to introduce additional structure.

The objective is:

- structural reduction,
- consistency formalization,
- recursive constraint definition,
- and admissibility clarification.

The axioms below do not yet constitute a complete theory.

They define provisional structural conditions for recursively admissible evolution.

---

# 2. Conceptual Role of Admissibility

Within TIG, admissibility is currently interpreted as:

> a structural selection condition restricting recursively inconsistent evolution.

Admissibility is not currently interpreted as:

- a physical force,
- a material substance,
- a quantum field,
- or a metaphysical principle.

The framework remains exploratory.

---

# 3. Minimal Structural Objective

The admissibility program attempts to answer:

> Under which minimal conditions can recursive structural evolution remain self-consistent?

The following axioms represent provisional constraints toward that objective.

---

# 4. Admissibility Axioms

---

# Axiom A1 — Recursive Consistency Preservation

A recursively evolving structure must preserve internal structural consistency across admissible recursive transitions.

Formally:

If:

\[
S_n \rightarrow S_{n+1}
\]

is admissible, then:

\[
C(S_n, S_{n+1}) \geq 0
\]

where:

- \(S_n\) denotes recursive state \(n\),
- and \(C\) denotes a consistency functional.

---

## Interpretation

Admissible recursive evolution may transform structure,
but may not generate internal contradiction.

---

## Current Status

Exploratory.

No finalized consistency functional currently exists.

---

# Axiom A2 — Bounded Recursive Evolution

Recursive admissible evolution must remain structurally bounded.

Formally:

\[
\sup_n ||S_n|| < \infty
\]

for admissible recursive sequences.

---

## Interpretation

Admissible recursive systems may evolve,
but may not diverge without bound.

---

## Motivation

This axiom attempts to prevent:

- uncontrolled recursive instability,
- pathological branch explosion,
- and structurally undefined evolution.

---

## Current Status

Exploratory.

No proof currently exists that boundedness is universally preserved.

---

# Axiom A3 — Admissible Branch Continuity

Admissible recursive branch transitions must preserve structural continuity.

Formally:

If:

\[
S_n \rightarrow S_{n+1}
\]

is admissible, then recursive transition operators must satisfy continuity constraints:

\[
\mathcal{T}(S_n) \sim S_{n+1}
\]

under admissibility-preserving evolution.

---

## Interpretation

Admissible evolution may branch,
but may not introduce discontinuous structural violation.

---

## Current Status

Heuristic.

No finalized topology on recursive branch space currently exists.

---

# Axiom A4 — Non-Pathological Recursive Closure

Admissible recursive systems must avoid self-destructive recursive closure.

---

## Interpretation

Recursive admissibility may permit recursion,
but not recursive collapse into structural inconsistency.

Examples of excluded behavior may include:

- contradictory recursive states,
- undefined recursive loops,
- singular recursive divergence,
- or branch structures violating admissibility preservation.

---

## Current Status

Exploratory.

No complete classification of pathological recursion currently exists.

---

# Axiom A5 — Classical Compatibility Limit

Admissibility-preserving recursive structures must recover classical General Relativity in the appropriate low-recursion limit.

In particular:

\[
\beta \rightarrow 0
\]

must recover the Schwarzschild sector.

---

## Interpretation

TIG may extend classical structure,
but may not invalidate the empirically verified GR limit.

---

## Current Status

Partially satisfied within current horizon formalism.

No complete covariant proof currently exists.

---

# 5. Structural Interpretation

The axioms above do not yet define dynamics.

They define only:

- admissibility constraints,
- recursive consistency requirements,
- and bounded structural evolution conditions.

This distinction is critical.

The framework currently prioritizes:

> admissible state selection before dynamical generation.
