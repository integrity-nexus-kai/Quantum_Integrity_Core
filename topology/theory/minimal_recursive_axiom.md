# MINIMAL RECURSIVE AXIOMS
## Minimal Structural Basis for Recursive TIG Topology

Version 0.1  
Status: Foundational Condensation Draft

---

# PURPOSE

This document defines the current minimal recursive axioms underlying the exploratory TIG topology framework.

The objective is not maximal descriptive power, but minimal structurally admissible theoretical foundations.

The framework attempts to reduce recursive topology toward the smallest coherent set of admissibility-preserving principles.

---

# AXIOM 1 — STRUCTURAL ADMISSIBILITY

Not every mathematically definable structure is physically or recursively admissible.

A structure is admissible only if it preserves recursive consistency under structural evolution.

Admissibility precedes interpretation.

---

# AXIOM 2 — RECURSIVE CONSISTENCY

Recursive evolution must preserve structural coherence across admissible transitions.

A recursive transformation that destroys consistency invalidates the resulting state.

Recursive instability therefore acts as a structural exclusion principle.

---

# AXIOM 3 — BOUNDED EVOLUTION

Recursive systems cannot evolve through unbounded structural divergence.

All admissible recursive dynamics must remain bounded within recursively stable regions of state-space.

This does not imply static equilibrium.

It implies constrained recursive evolution.

---

# AXIOM 4 — GEOMETRIC DEPENDENCE

Recursive admissibility depends on geometric structure.

Geometry is not externally imposed.

Instead, recursive geometry emerges from admissibility-preserving structural relations.

The recursive manifold therefore represents admissible geometric organization.

---

# AXIOM 5 — MINIMAL RECURSIVE COMPLEXITY

Recursive structure should evolve toward minimal admissible complexity.

Additional structures require structural necessity.

Conceptual proliferation without recursive necessity is prohibited.

---

# AXIOM 6 — STABILITY PRIORITY

Stable recursive configurations possess structural preference over unstable configurations.

Recursive instability acts as a suppressive mechanism on admissibility.

The framework therefore assumes recursive stability selection.

---

# AXIOM 7 — REDUCTION PRIORITY

A stronger theoretical structure should reduce the number of independent assumptions.

Theoretical refinement should:

- reduce redundancy,
- improve coherence,
- and preserve recursive admissibility.

Expansion alone is not considered progress.

---

# AXIOM 8 — STRUCTURAL EMERGENCE

Higher-order recursive structures should emerge from lower admissibility relations whenever possible.

The framework prefers emergence over independent postulation.

---

# AXIOM 9 — INTERPRETATIONAL RESTRAINT

Interpretation must remain weaker than formal structure.

Physical meaning should not be assigned unless supported by mathematically coherent recursive relations.

The framework therefore prioritizes formal consistency over speculative ontology.

---

# AXIOM 10 — BASELINE COMPATIBILITY

The recursive topology framework should preserve compatibility with the TIG baseline structure.

No recursive extension may invalidate the core admissibility behavior established by the original TIG framework without rigorous justification.

---

# CURRENT STATUS

The present axioms remain exploratory.

They should be viewed as condensation candidates rather than finalized foundational laws.

Future reduction may:

- merge axioms,
- eliminate redundancy,
- derive hidden dependencies,
- or reveal deeper minimal structure.

---

# LONG-TERM OBJECTIVE

The long-term objective is the derivation of:

- recursive geometric structure,
- admissibility-preserving dynamics,
- bounded recursive evolution,
- and recursive stability behavior

from the smallest mathematically coherent set of admissible principles.

---

# NEXT STEP

The next theoretical phase should focus on:

- dependency reduction between axioms,
- derivation of minimal recursive equations,
- admissibility operators,
- and formal recursive geometric structure.

Further uncontrolled axiom proliferation should be avoided.
