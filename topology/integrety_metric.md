# INTEGRITY METRIC
## Quantification Framework for Structural Consistency in TIG Topology

Version 0.1  
Status: Foundational Draft

---

# 1. PURPOSE

This document introduces the preliminary concept of an integrity metric within the Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) topology framework.

The integrity metric is intended to provide a structural measure for evaluating:

- admissibility stability,
- recursive consistency,
- topology preservation,
- and bounded geometric evolution.

The objective is not yet a complete numerical metric formalism, but a foundational framework for describing relative structural integrity across admissible state space.

---

# 2. MOTIVATION

Previous TIG topology layers define:

- admissible states,
- forbidden states,
- transition operators,
- critical surfaces,
- and recursive consistency conditions.

However, no formal mechanism yet exists for evaluating:

> how structurally stable or consistent a state actually is.

The integrity metric is introduced to quantify structural admissibility behavior.

---

# 3. BASIC INTERPRETATION

The integrity metric assigns a structural consistency value to a geometric state or transition.

Symbolically:

\[
\mathcal{I}(S)
\]

where:

- \(S\) denotes a geometric configuration,
- and \(\mathcal{I}\) denotes structural integrity.

The integrity metric is interpreted as a measure of:

- recursive stability,
- admissibility preservation,
- and topology compatibility.

---

# 4. PRELIMINARY PROPERTIES

The integrity metric should satisfy the following preliminary conditions.

---

## 4.1 Boundedness

Admissible states possess finite integrity values.

Symbolically:

\[
0 \leq \mathcal{I}(S) < \infty
\]

Unbounded integrity divergence indicates structural instability.

---

## 4.2 Recursive Compatibility

Integrity must remain compatible with recursive consistency conditions.

States generating recursive contradiction possess reduced admissibility integrity.

---

## 4.3 Topology Sensitivity

Integrity depends on proximity to critical topology boundaries.

Near:

\[
\Delta = 0
\]

integrity stability may weaken due to transition sensitivity.

---

## 4.4 Transition Stability

Admissible evolution preserves integrity continuity across transitions.

Discontinuous integrity collapse indicates forbidden structural evolution.

---

# 5. STATE INTERPRETATION

Different regions of TIG topology may correspond to different integrity behavior.

Examples include:

| Region | Integrity Behavior |
|---|---|
| stable horizon region | high structural integrity |
| critical transition region | reduced stability |
| admissible compact region | bounded integrity |
| forbidden region | integrity breakdown |

The integrity metric therefore organizes structural stability across state space.

---

# 6. RELATION TO ADMISSIBILITY

Admissibility conditions determine whether a state is structurally allowed.

The integrity metric estimates how stable that admissibility remains.

Thus:

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| admissibility conditions | define allowance |
| integrity metric | define stability quality |

Admissibility and integrity therefore act as complementary structural layers.

---

# 7. RELATION TO ROOT MANIFOLD

The root manifold defines geometric continuity.

The integrity metric defines structural stability along manifold trajectories.

Transitions through highly unstable regions may approach forbidden topology boundaries.

---

# 8. RELATION TO TRANSITION OPERATORS

Transition operators may preserve or reduce integrity.

An admissible operator should satisfy:

\[
\mathcal{I}(S_j) \approx \mathcal{I}(S_i)
\]

within bounded structural deviation.

Operators generating integrity collapse become non-admissible.

---

# 9. RELATION TO X4

X4 parametrizes movement through structural state space.

The integrity metric may vary as a function of X4 evolution.

Thus:

- X4 defines transition direction,
- integrity defines structural stability during traversal.

This interpretation suggests that some transition regions may become structurally unstable before forbidden topology is reached.

---

# 10. RELATION TO SIR

The integrity metric provides one of the strongest current mathematical bridges between TIG topology and Structural Integrity Recursion (SIR).

Within SIR:

- integrity acts as a recursive preservation principle.

Within TIG topology:

- integrity becomes a measurable structural stability condition.

This suggests that recursive stability may eventually admit quantitative geometric representation.

The integrity metric may later correspond to:

No complete quantum formalism is proposed at the present stage.

Within TIG topology:

The integrity metric therefore represents the first candidate quantification framework for recursive topology-preserving geometric consistency in TIG.
