# INTEGRITY COLLAPSE
## Structural Failure and Admissibility Breakdown in TIG Topology

Version 0.1  
Status: Foundational Draft

---

# 1. PURPOSE

This document defines the preliminary concept of integrity collapse within the Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG) topology framework.

Integrity collapse describes the structural breakdown of:

- admissibility,
- recursive consistency,
- topology continuity,
- or bounded geometric evolution.

The objective is to formalize how recursive geometric systems lose structural stability and transition toward forbidden configurations.

---

# 2. MOTIVATION

Previous TIG topology documents define:

- admissible evolution,
- recursive dynamics,
- integrity metrics,
- transition operators,
- and topology-preserving state traversal.

However, no formal framework yet exists for describing:

> how admissible systems become structurally unstable.

Integrity collapse is introduced to define structural failure within TIG topology.

---

# 3. CORE PRINCIPLE

Within TIG:

> structural integrity is not guaranteed.

Recursive geometric systems remain admissible only while:

- recursive consistency,
- bounded evolution,
- topology continuity,
- and integrity preservation

remain satisfied.

Violation of these conditions may generate integrity collapse.

---

# 4. BASIC COLLAPSE CONDITION

Integrity collapse occurs when recursive evolution can no longer preserve admissibility.

Symbolically:

\[
\mathcal{I}(S_n) \rightarrow 0
\]

or when:

\[
\mathcal{R}(S_n \rightarrow S_{n+1}) = \text{inconsistent}
\]

where:

- \(\mathcal{I}\) denotes integrity,
- and \(\mathcal{R}\) denotes recursive consistency.

---

# 5. PRIMARY COLLAPSE MODES

Several collapse modes are currently proposed.

---

## 5.1 Recursive Collapse

Occurs when recursive evolution generates internal structural contradiction.

Examples include:

- incompatible recursive states,
- unstable recursion cycles,
- or self-destructive transition structure.

---

## 5.2 Topology Collapse

Occurs when topology continuity cannot be preserved.

Examples include:

- branch disconnection,
- manifold fracture,
- or forbidden topology transition.

---

## 5.3 Critical Instability Collapse

Occurs near critical surfaces where transition sensitivity becomes excessive.

Near:

\[
\Delta = 0
\]

small recursive deviations may trigger large structural instability.

---

## 5.4 Divergence Collapse

Occurs when recursive evolution becomes unbounded.

Symbolically:

\[
|E(S)| \rightarrow \infty
\]

Unbounded structural divergence violates admissibility.

---

# 6. FORBIDDEN STATES

Integrity collapse may generate forbidden states.

Forbidden states correspond to configurations violating:

- recursive consistency,
- topology continuity,
- bounded evolution,
- or structural integrity preservation.

Forbidden regions are interpreted as physically inaccessible.

---

# 7. RELATION TO ADMISSIBILITY

Admissibility conditions determine allowed recursive evolution.

Integrity collapse defines the failure of admissibility preservation.

Thus:

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| admissibility | structural allowance |
| integrity collapse | structural failure |

---

# 8. RELATION TO RECURSIVE DYNAMICS

Recursive dynamics define constrained geometric evolution.

Integrity collapse defines the breakdown of recursive stability during evolution.

Collapse therefore acts as the instability boundary of recursive dynamics.

---

# 9. RELATION TO ROOT MANIFOLD

The root manifold defines continuous admissible geometry.

Integrity collapse may generate:

- disconnected branches,
- unstable manifold regions,
- or forbidden traversal pathways.

Collapse therefore threatens manifold continuity.

---

# 10. RELATION TO TRANSITION OPERATORS

Transition operators remain admissible only while integrity is preserved.

Operators generating:

- recursive inconsistency,
- topology fracture,
- or divergence

become collapse-inducing operators.

---

# 11. RELATION TO X4

X4 parametrizes traversal through recursive state space.

Integrity collapse may occur when X4 evolution approaches forbidden transition regions.

Thus:

- X4 defines traversal direction,
- integrity collapse defines traversal failure.

---

# 12. RELATION TO GENERAL RELATIVITY

Within standard General Relativity, singularities represent regions where classical geometric description fails.

Within TIG topology:

- integrity collapse may reinterpret singularity formation as recursive admissibility failure.

This suggests that topology-preserving bounded evolution may replace unconstrained singular collapse.

No complete singularity replacement formalism is proposed at the present stage.

---

# 13. RELATION TO SIR

Integrity collapse provides one of the strongest conceptual bridges between TIG topology and Structural Integrity Recursion (SIR).

Within SIR:

- recursive corruption generates instability.

Within TIG:

- integrity collapse generates forbidden geometric evolution.

This suggests that recursive instability may represent a universal structural failure mechanism.

Integrity collapse may later correspond to:

No complete quantum formalism is proposed at the present stage.

Within TIG topology:

- admissibility defines allowed evolution,
- integrity defines structural stability,

- and integrity collapse defines structural failure.

Integrity collapse therefore represents the first formal instability framework within TIG topology.
