# GLOSSARY
## Terminology Framework for Topological Integrity Gravity (TIG)

Version 0.1  
Status: Foundational Definitions

---

# 1. PURPOSE

This glossary defines the core terminology used throughout the TIG topology framework.

The objective is to establish:

- conceptual consistency,
- mathematical clarity,
- structural coherence,
- and stable terminology for future formalization.

The definitions below are preliminary and may evolve with future mathematical development.

---

# 2. INTEGRITY

## Definition

Integrity is the preservation of admissible structural evolution within a constrained geometric state space.

Integrity is not interpreted as:

- morality,
- software correctness,
- or information completeness.

Within TIG, integrity describes:

> the preservation of recursive geometric consistency across state transitions.

---

# 3. ADMISSIBLE STATE

## Definition

An admissible state is a geometric configuration satisfying all active structural consistency constraints.

An admissible state preserves:

- recursive consistency,
- bounded evolution,
- topological continuity,
- and discriminant compatibility.

Admissible states belong to the physically accessible region of the topology framework.

---

# 4. FORBIDDEN STATE

## Definition

A forbidden state is a geometric configuration violating structural consistency conditions.

Forbidden states may include:

- singular collapse states,
- recursive breakdown,
- topological inconsistency,
- or structurally disconnected transitions.

Forbidden states are interpreted as non-physical or structurally inaccessible.

---

# 5. RECURSIVE CONSISTENCY

## Definition

Recursive consistency describes the requirement that geometric evolution remains structurally self-compatible across transition sequences.

A recursively consistent evolution:

- preserves admissibility,
- avoids structural contradiction,
- and maintains bounded transition continuity.

Recursive consistency acts as a global stability condition on spacetime evolution.

---

# 6. X4

## Definition

X4 is a structural transition coordinate governing admissible movement through recursive geometric state space.

X4 is not currently interpreted as:

- a conventional spatial dimension,
- a compactified dimension,
- or a directly observable coordinate.

Instead, X4 parametrizes:

- transition direction,
- branch evolution,
- topological regime change,
- and admissibility flow.

---

# 7. TRANSITION GEOMETRY

## Definition

Transition geometry describes the structural organization of admissible state changes within TIG.

The concept includes:

- branch movement,
- critical transitions,
- root evolution,
- discriminant crossings,
- and topology-preserving evolution.

Transition geometry studies how spacetime changes between admissible configurations.

---

# 8. CRITICAL BOUNDARY

## Definition

A critical boundary is a structural transition surface separating distinct geometric regimes.

Within TIG, the primary critical boundary is associated with:

\[
\Delta = 0
\]

where the discriminant changes sign.

Critical boundaries separate:

- horizon-supporting states,
- critical states,
- and non-horizon states.

---

# 9. DISCRIMINANT SURFACE

## Definition

The discriminant surface is the set of parameter configurations for which the cubic horizon equation undergoes root degeneracy.

It defines the topological transition region of the TIG framework.

The discriminant surface acts as:

- a bifurcation boundary,
- a stability separator,
- and a transition manifold candidate.

---

# 10. CUBE STRUCTURE

## Definition

The cube structure is a structural coordinate model organizing admissible TIG configurations.

The cube is not a physical object.

It is an abstract state-space representation used to classify:

- admissible regions,
- forbidden regions,
- transition surfaces,
- and structural evolution paths.

---

# 11. TOPOLOGICAL ADMISSIBILITY

## Definition

Topological admissibility describes whether a state transition preserves global structural consistency.

A topologically admissible transition:

- remains inside the allowed state manifold,
- preserves recursive continuity,
- and avoids forbidden structural crossings.

---

# 12. HORIZON TRANSITION

## Definition

A horizon transition is a structural change between:

- horizon-supporting configurations
and
- non-horizon configurations.

Within TIG, horizon formation depends not only on metric curvature, but also on topological admissibility conditions.

---

# 13. ROOT STRUCTURE

## Definition

Root structure describes the arrangement and evolution of solutions generated by the TIG cubic equation.

Changes in root structure correspond to:

- critical transitions,
- bifurcation events,
- and topology changes in admissible state space.

---

# 14. STRUCTURAL REGULARIZATION

## Definition

Structural regularization is the bounded evolution mechanism preventing uncontrolled geometric divergence.

The concept suggests that spacetime evolution may terminate in admissible bounded states before singular breakdown occurs.

---

# 15. STATE SPACE

## Definition

State space is the total set of geometrically possible configurations considered within the TIG framework.

Only a subset of state space is physically admissible.

The admissible subset is determined by:

- recursive consistency,
- discriminant constraints,
- topological continuity,
- and integrity preservation.

---

# 16. CURRENT STATUS

All definitions in this glossary are preliminary.

The terminology framework is intended to stabilize conceptual language before complete mathematical formalization.

Future work may introduce:

- tensor definitions,
- manifold formalization,
- operator notation,
- and explicit topology metrics.
